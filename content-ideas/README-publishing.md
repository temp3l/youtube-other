Based on your answers, **Dark Truth Episodes should launch as an English-first, AI-assisted horror storytelling brand with three long videos per week and Shorts derived from the same production pipeline.**

Your production costs are low enough to test aggressively, but the main risk is not financial—it is making the channel look repetitive or mass-produced. YouTube permits AI-assisted content, but monetization still depends on originality, authenticity, and sufficient variation between videos. ([Google Help][1])

# Recommended positioning

Use this channel promise:

> **Disturbing stories, unexplained cases, recovered recordings, and dark events that may—or may not—be true.**

This fits your mixture of fiction, real cases, and urban legends without forcing every episode into the same format.

Use four clearly labeled content types:

| Series                   | Content                                              | Recommended share |
| ------------------------ | ---------------------------------------------------- | ----------------: |
| **Recovered Files**      | Fictional recordings, reports and surveillance cases |               35% |
| **Rules After Midnight** | Rules horror and workplace/night-shift stories       |               25% |
| **Dark True Cases**      | Documented events and mysteries                      |               25% |
| **Urban Legends**        | Folklore and localized legends                       |               15% |

For real cases, distinguish fact from dramatization. Never present an invented detail as verified evidence.

# Recommendation on darkness and graphic content

Choose **psychologically dark, visually non-graphic horror**.

This is the best balance for reach, monetization, production cost, and repeat viewing.

## Use freely

- Unseen threats
- Distorted silhouettes
- Empty rooms
- Strange recordings
- Missing-person mysteries
- Possession and paranormal themes
- Implied deaths
- Bloodless crime scenes
- Isolation and paranoia
- Disturbing final revelations
- Limited, non-detailed blood when narratively necessary

## Use sparingly

- Visible injuries
- Crime-scene photographs
- Detailed descriptions of torture
- Suicide themes
- Child victims
- Real emergency recordings
- Recent real-world murders
- Thumbnails implying mutilation

## Avoid

- Gore as the main attraction
- Severed body parts
- Graphic corpses
- Explicit torture imagery
- Graphic harm involving children
- Shock thumbnails
- Repeated screaming or suffering without narrative context

Non-graphic dramatized controversial material can qualify for advertising under YouTube’s updated guidelines, while content focused on graphic violence may be restricted, age-gated, or receive limited advertising. ([Google Help][2])

My recommended rule:

> **Make the narration disturbing enough that viewers imagine the horror; do not show what their imagination can create more effectively.**

# Video structure for a ten-minute episode

At a fast narration speed around 175–185 words per minute, target roughly **1,550–1,750 spoken words**. Leave space for pauses, sound effects, and visual beats.

Use this structure:

| Time       | Purpose                           |
| ---------- | --------------------------------- |
| 0:00–0:15  | Disturbing cold open              |
| 0:15–0:45  | Establish the mystery             |
| 0:45–2:00  | Character and setting             |
| 2:00–4:30  | First escalation                  |
| 4:30–6:30  | Evidence or rule violation        |
| 6:30–8:30  | Main reveal                       |
| 8:30–9:30  | Consequence                       |
| 9:30–10:00 | Final twist and next-video bridge |

Every 30–60 seconds, introduce at least one new element:

- Evidence
- Contradiction
- Threat
- Rule
- Discovery
- Reversal
- Question

Do not artificially stretch a five-minute story into ten minutes. Publish it at five or six minutes when that is its natural length.

# Static-image production strategy

Static images are workable, but they cannot feel like a slideshow.

For a ten-minute episode, I recommend:

- **35–55 unique primary images**
- Average scene duration of roughly **8–15 seconds**
- Additional crops, detail shots, overlays, and movement derived from those images
- More frequent changes during the opening and climax

Apply movement in editing:

- Slow push-in
- Horizontal pan
- Foreground/background parallax
- Focus shifts
- Light flicker
- Subtle handheld motion
- Surveillance timestamps
- Redactions and document overlays
- Shadow movement
- Glitch effects used sparingly

Do not generate one image per spoken sentence. It increases cost and often makes visual continuity worse.

Each recurring character should have a locked description covering:

- Approximate age
- Hair
- Clothing
- Face shape
- Distinctive features
- Color palette
- Lighting style

Your `scenes.json` should preserve those attributes across every prompt.

# AI voice recommendation

Use one consistent, natural adult narrator voice for at least the first 30 episodes.

Recommended delivery:

- Approximately **175–185 words per minute**
- Calm and credible rather than theatrical
- Slightly lower energy during setup
- Faster during escalation
- Controlled pauses before major revelations
- No exaggerated “scary voice”
- No shouting unless the source recording requires it

The channel should sound like a serious case narrator describing something impossible.

Add occasional secondary processed voices for:

- Emergency calls
- Voicemails
- Radio broadcasts
- Police interviews
- Recovered recordings

Do not use a different main narrator for every episode.

# Real cases and copyright safety

For fictional episodes:

- Mark them as fictional or dramatized in the description.
- Do not falsely attribute them to real police departments or identifiable living people.
- Avoid realistic fake allegations against real people.

For true cases:

- Research from multiple reliable sources.
- Write an original script.
- Do not closely paraphrase another YouTube video.
- Do not download and reuse another creator’s visuals or narration.
- Use short source excerpts only when justified.
- Keep a source list.
- Avoid unresolved accusations against private individuals.
- Clearly label reconstructions.

For urban legends:

- Research the broader legend rather than rewriting one creator’s telling.
- Add original framing, interpretation, localization, or investigation.
- Identify known variations of the legend.

# Thumbnail system

Use three reusable layouts, not one rigid template.

## Layout A: Evidence

- Document, tape, photograph, phone, or camera
- One highlighted anomaly
- Optional timestamp
- Two to four words

## Layout B: Threat

- Person in foreground
- Figure or danger in background
- Clear visual separation
- No visual clutter

## Layout C: Forbidden place

- Door, tunnel, room, forest path, stairwell
- One impossible or threatening detail
- Strong central focal point

- **RULE 7**
- **DON’T OPEN IT**
- **HE CAME BACK**
- **FOUND INSIDE**
- **3:14 AM**

Do not place the entire title on the image.

# Shorts strategy

Each long video should produce up to three Short candidates:

1. **Self-contained adaptation**
2. **Unanswered teaser**
3. **Most disturbing reveal without spoiling the ending**

A Short should not say only “watch the full video.” It must provide its own entertainment value.

Example:

> Every night at 2:14, Daniel’s security camera recorded him returning home. That would not have been strange—except Daniel had disappeared six days earlier. On the seventh night, the camera showed him standing at his door. He never entered. He only looked directly into the camera and whispered, “Don’t let me in.”

Then end with:

> The full recording was recovered from Apartment 14.

# Localization plan

Do not localize every English video immediately.

Your additional-language cost is low, but translation also creates operational overhead and may scale weak concepts.

Use this sequence:

1. Publish 15–20 English episodes.
2. Wait for useful retention and click data.
3. Select the five best performers.
4. Localize those five into one test language.
5. Use separate language channels.
6. Localize thumbnails, titles, narration, descriptions, and on-screen text.
7. Avoid literal translation of hooks and idioms.

Your first test language should probably be **German or Spanish**, depending on which localization quality you can review most confidently.
