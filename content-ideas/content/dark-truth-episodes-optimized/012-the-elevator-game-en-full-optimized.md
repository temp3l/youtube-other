# Narration Script

Emma completed the button sequence exactly. On the tenth floor, the doors opened onto a city with no lights and a red sky.

Emma Shaw was a graduate student producing a podcast about urban rituals. She chose a twenty-storey apartment tower scheduled for renovation because its elevator still used mechanical relays and because residents had reported chimes sounding from floors the building did not contain.

At first, the detail seemed small enough to dismiss. The ritual required her to ride alone after midnight and press a sequence of floors without leaving the car. Emma mounted a camera above the control panel, started an audio recorder and told her producer she would stop at the first malfunction. The setting offered no obvious sign of danger, which made the warning easy to treat as superstition rather than procedure. The character paused long enough to test the obvious explanation, but the environment answered before reassurance could settle in. A familiar detail was now wrong in a way that could be checked rather than merely felt.

That explanation lasted only until the next night. At the fifth floor, a woman entered. She wore a grey coat and stood with her face toward the corner. The rules said Emma must not speak to her, answer her questions or look directly at her. The woman pressed no button, yet the doors closed. The repeated detail mattered because it returned at a precise moment, not as a vague memory after the event. The reaction was practical rather than reckless: check the exit, verify the time, preserve a recording and avoid touching what had changed. None of those precautions restored the earlier version of events.

From that point, the pattern stopped looking accidental. The elevator began moving upward while the display counted backward: eight, seven, six. Emma felt the pressure of ascent in her ears. The woman asked, very softly, 'Where are you going?' Emma watched only the reflection in the metal doors. Instead of escalating immediately, the threat waited for a human response and then adapted to it. Silence followed for several seconds. That pause mattered because it gave everyone present time to understand the same thing independently: the incident had responded to them.

The first real warning came when the ordinary rules failed. When the display reached ten, the doors opened onto the lobby of a city that resembled Emma's own. Every window was dark. No traffic moved. Above the rooftops hung a red sky without stars, and a single light blinked from a tower several kilometres away. The sound motif returned—an elevator motor, a floor chime, and fluorescent lights buzzing—but now it came from a place where no ordinary source could be found. No dramatic attack followed immediately. Instead, the setting became less dependable—distance, sound, reflections or locked boundaries no longer behaved as expected.

What followed changed the meaning of everything before it. Her phone showed messages timestamped three hours in the future. They had been sent from her own account: DO NOT RETURN IN THE ELEVATOR. SHE IS NOT FOLLOWING YOU. SHE IS WAITING THERE. Each time Emma reread them, the wording changed slightly. This was the point at which observation replaced disbelief. The evidence did not explain the event; it only proved that the earlier incidents belonged to the same design. The evidence created a worse problem than disbelief. Once the detail had been confirmed, every ordinary decision carried the risk of satisfying a rule nobody fully understood.

The danger then became personal. The woman stepped out behind her. Under the red light, Emma finally saw her face. It was Emma's face, older, exhausted and marked by a long cut across the forehead. The woman claimed she had completed the ritual months earlier and had failed the return sequence. The rule was narrow enough to offer hope, but cruel enough that one emotional mistake could break it. The safest choice was also the hardest one emotionally. The threat had learned which voice, memory or promise could make caution feel cruel.

A reasonable attempt to escape made the situation worse. Only one passenger could leave through the elevator. The older Emma begged for Emma's place, insisting that the city copied anyone who remained too long. Across the empty street, figures began appearing in windows. Every figure looked like Emma at a different age. The attempted solution did not erase the danger. It revealed what the danger had been waiting for. They acted quickly, but the result showed that speed alone was useless. The danger was not chasing their bodies; it was steering their decisions.

For a few minutes, the plan appeared to work. Emma re-entered the elevator but refused to complete either sequence. She stopped the car between floors using the emergency switch, climbed through the roof hatch and followed the service ladder while the older version pounded beneath her. The temporary silence created the most dangerous moment, because it encouraged the survivors to believe the worst had passed. For a moment, ordinary background noise returned and the space looked normal again. That false calm allowed the next change to arrive without warning.

The apparent ending did not survive until morning. The shaft should have been twenty storeys high. Emma climbed past floor numbers thirty-one, forty-six and seventy. She eventually found a maintenance door labelled GROUND, although the shaft continued both above and below it. What remained could be photographed or recorded, but not interpreted in a way that made the survivors feel safe. Later investigators could confirm isolated details, but not the chain connecting them. The survivor was left with proof strong enough to remember and incomplete enough to doubt.

The final piece of evidence arrived later. She opened the door and stepped into the real building at sunrise. Her producer found the elevator parked on the tenth floor. The camera showed Emma entering at midnight, but it never showed her leaving. Beside her in every frame stood the woman in grey. That final detail transformed survival from an ending into another stage of the pattern. Nothing in the final record explained how the pattern continued. It only showed that the survivor's method of escape had become part of the threat's next approach.

Before the final decision, the surviving record describes a deliberate verification attempt. Times were compared, exits were checked and the source of an elevator motor, a floor chime, and fluorescent lights buzzing was traced as far as the setting allowed. Each check eliminated one ordinary explanation but also consumed time. The characters were not waiting passively for another scare; they were learning that the phenomenon became more precise whenever they tried to measure it. What first appeared to be atmosphere had become a signalling system.

Three days later, Emma received a scheduled message from herself. It contained a photograph taken inside the elevator that morning. Emma was visible outside the closed doors. The person holding the camera was standing inside, waiting for her to press the call button.
