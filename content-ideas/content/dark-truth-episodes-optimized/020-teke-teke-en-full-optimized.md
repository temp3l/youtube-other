# Narration Script

The sound began behind Nina after the last train passed: teke... teke... teke. Every time she ran, it matched her speed.

Nina Carter was a documentary photographer visiting a quiet railway district in northern Japan. She had come to photograph an old crossing associated with a local story about a woman killed on the tracks.

At first, the detail seemed small enough to dismiss. A station worker warned Nina to leave before the final freight train. He pointed to a missing memorial plaque beside the crossing and said the story had changed after someone stole it. Nina assumed he was trying to frighten tourists. The setting offered no obvious sign of danger, which made the warning easy to treat as superstition rather than procedure. The character paused long enough to test the obvious explanation, but the environment answered before reassurance could settle in. A familiar detail was now wrong in a way that could be checked rather than merely felt.

That explanation lasted only until the next night. After midnight, the barriers lowered though no train was scheduled. A bell rang once. From beneath the platform came a rapid scraping sound, like hard objects striking concrete in alternating beats. The repeated detail mattered because it returned at a precise moment, not as a vague memory after the event. The reaction was practical rather than reckless: check the exit, verify the time, preserve a recording and avoid touching what had changed. None of those precautions restored the earlier version of events.

From that point, the pattern stopped looking accidental. Nina aimed her camera into the darkness. The flash revealed a woman pulling herself forward with both arms. Below the waist there was nothing. The figure moved faster than a person could run, producing the rhythm that gave the legend its name. Instead of escalating immediately, the threat waited for a human response and then adapted to it. Silence followed for several seconds. That pause mattered because it gave everyone present time to understand the same thing independently: the incident had responded to them.

The first real warning came when the ordinary rules failed. The apparition asked Nina where her legs were. The station worker had warned that hesitation or the wrong answer would bring an attack. Nina replied that she did not know and threw the camera flash directly toward the woman's face. The sound motif returned—a rail-crossing bell, rapid scraping, and a distant train horn—but now it came from a place where no ordinary source could be found. No dramatic attack followed immediately. Instead, the setting became less dependable—distance, sound, reflections or locked boundaries no longer behaved as expected.

What followed changed the meaning of everything before it. She ran through service alleys while the scraping matched every change in speed. Mirrors and dark windows showed the woman much closer than she appeared when Nina looked back. In one reflection, the figure was already holding Nina's ankle. This was the point at which observation replaced disbelief. The evidence did not explain the event; it only proved that the earlier incidents belonged to the same design. The evidence created a worse problem than disbelief. Once the detail had been confirmed, every ordinary decision carried the risk of satisfying a rule nobody fully understood.

The danger then became personal. Nina reached another crossing and noticed the spirit repeated the route of the original accident. Every pursuit ended at the same section of track. The missing memorial had not protected travellers; it had marked the point where the spirit was supposed to stop. The rule was narrow enough to offer hope, but cruel enough that one emotional mistake could break it. The safest choice was also the hardest one emotionally. The threat had learned which voice, memory or promise could make caution feel cruel.

A reasonable attempt to escape made the situation worse. Using archived photographs on her phone, Nina found the plaque discarded behind a maintenance shed. It carried the victim's name and the date of death. As the scraping approached, Nina replaced it beside the rail. The attempted solution did not erase the danger. It revealed what the danger had been waiting for. They acted quickly, but the result showed that speed alone was useless. The danger was not chasing their bodies; it was steering their decisions.

For a few minutes, the plan appeared to work. The woman stopped at the edge of the memorial. For the first time, her face appeared less angry than confused. Nina spoke the victim's name and told her where the missing half of her body had been buried after the accident. The temporary silence created the most dangerous moment, because it encouraged the survivors to believe the worst had passed. For a moment, ordinary background noise returned and the space looked normal again. That false calm allowed the next change to arrive without warning.

The apparent ending did not survive until morning. A freight train passed between them. When it cleared, the platform was empty. Police later found Nina unconscious beside the restored plaque. Her camera contained hundreds of photographs taken during the chase, but every image showed Nina running alone. What remained could be photographed or recorded, but not interpreted in a way that made the survivors feel safe. Later investigators could confirm isolated details, but not the chain connecting them. The survivor was left with proof strong enough to remember and incomplete enough to doubt.

The final piece of evidence arrived later. The final photograph was different. It had been taken from ground level behind Nina. Two pale hands were visible at the bottom of the frame, pulling the camera forward. That final detail transformed survival from an ending into another stage of the pattern. Nothing in the final record explained how the pattern continued. It only showed that the survivor's method of escape had become part of the threat's next approach.

Before the final decision, the surviving record describes a deliberate verification attempt. Times were compared, exits were checked and the source of a rail-crossing bell, rapid scraping, and a distant train horn was traced as far as the setting allowed. Each check eliminated one ordinary explanation but also consumed time. The characters were not waiting passively for another scare; they were learning that the phenomenon became more precise whenever they tried to measure it. What first appeared to be atmosphere had become a signalling system.

Nina survived and published none of the images. She still hears the scraping whenever she waits near railway tracks after midnight. It no longer follows behind her. It begins ahead, as though something is returning along the route she escaped.
