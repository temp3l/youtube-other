# Narration Script

The doll had no eyes, yet every time Daniel turned away, its empty sockets were facing him.

Daniel Price was a antique dealer when the account began in a family estate filled with old toys.
The object at the centre of it was an eyeless wooden doll containing tiny photographs. It did not
arrive with violence, a body or a clear threat. It entered the story through routine work, which
gave Daniel every reason to remain calm. Later, that ordinary beginning became important because the
earliest details were the only ones that never changed.

The warning was already there: The estate seller said, 'Never look into both sockets at once.'
Daniel did what most careful people would do. The instruction was photographed, logged and treated
as a precaution left by someone superstitious. Nothing in the room appeared damaged. No alarm had
triggered. The doors, windows and locks were all in the condition described by the records.

Each socket held miniature photographs of previous owners instead of glass eyes. Daniel repeated the
test rather than trusting a first impression. Equipment was restarted, measurements were taken again
and timestamps were compared. The result remained the same. That repetition was more disturbing than
a sudden shock because it removed the easiest explanation: simple error.

For a while, the incident could still be contained. Daniel stepped away, returned later and asked
another person to observe without being told what to expect. That observer noticed a different
detail, but it pointed in the same direction. The account was no longer based on one frightened
person's memory.

People near the doll began losing details from their vision: colours, faces and finally depth. The
episode's recurring sounds—a toy mechanism, floorboards creaking, and a soft camera click—began
appearing only at the moment the pattern advanced. They stayed low in the background, never
announcing exactly what had changed. Their purpose was to make the audience recognise danger before
the characters did.

The pattern followed one rule: The doll saw through anyone who looked directly into it and stored
each stolen viewpoint as a photograph. Once Daniel understood that rule, the earlier incidents
stopped looking random. They formed a sequence: observation, response, escalation and correction.
The threat was testing how a reasonable person reacted and changing its behaviour after every failed
attempt to control it.

Daniel began an investigation rather than waiting for another incident. Old records, archived
messages, previous owners and maintenance reports were compared. The dates were separated by years,
sometimes decades, but the language was almost identical. Different witnesses had described the same
timing, the same repeated sound and the same moment when a harmless anomaly became personal.

The records also revealed what earlier victims had done wrong. Some tried to destroy the object
immediately. Others shared it, moved away or convinced themselves the pattern had ended. None of
those actions broke the rule. They only changed where the next incident occurred.

Daniel tried the safest option first. The source was isolated, the location was secured and an
outside contact was told exactly where to look if communication stopped. For several minutes, the
measure appeared successful. Then the recurring sound returned from a place it could not physically
reach.

At night, the photographs arranged themselves into a precise map of Daniel's home. This was the
first piece of evidence that survived outside the protagonist's perception. It could be
photographed, copied or compared. It did not prove a supernatural explanation, but it proved the
official explanation was incomplete.

The discovery also changed the emotional stakes. Until then, Daniel had been protecting a job, a
reputation or a piece of evidence. Now the threat had reached into personal history and selected
details designed to produce hesitation. Leaving was still possible, but leaving someone or something
behind was no longer simple.

Daniel's assistant could no longer recognise faces, while the doll's carved features became more
lifelike. Daniel responded with another practical measure: stopping the work, separating people from
the source and calling for help. The response failed in a specific way. The failure revealed that
distance alone would not end the pattern.

At this point, the account accelerated. Familiar exits became unreliable. Ordinary background noise
disappeared between incidents. Small details shifted position whenever nobody was watching. The
location seemed less like a haunted place and more like a mechanism closing around one person.

The only workable plan was this: Daniel sealed the sockets with wax and placed the doll in a
mirrored box so it could see only itself. It relied on observation instead of physical strength. The
goal was not to destroy something that did not obey ordinary limits. The goal was to force it into
repeating the one behaviour it could not change.

Before acting, Daniel tested the plan on a smaller scale. The result created a brief opening and
confirmed the rule, but it also warned the threat. From that moment forward, every sound and
movement arrived faster. The entity no longer waited to be noticed.

The mirrors filled with hundreds of photographed eyes. The doll began turning inside the sealed
case, and Daniel's vision narrowed to two circular tunnels. He rotated the mirrors until every
reflection pointed back into one socket, forcing the stolen images to collapse inward. Daniel had
only one opportunity to commit to the plan. Reversing course would have repeated the earlier mistake
and strengthened the trap. The final action worked not because it was powerful, but because it
contradicted what the threat expected a frightened person to do.

For several seconds, the location became completely silent. The recurring motif stopped. Lights,
clocks or electronic displays returned to normal. Daniel did not interpret that silence as victory.
It felt more like the moment after a machine had finished recording.

The missing vision returned and the doll became still. A week later, Daniel found his own photograph
inside a socket that remained sealed with unbroken wax. The image showed him standing behind
himself.

In the hours after the escape, Daniel documented everything while the sequence was still fresh. The
notes were duplicated, timestamps were preserved and physical evidence was stored separately. By
morning, several details had already changed. A name was missing from one copy. A time differed by
one minute. A photograph showed an object in the wrong position.

Investigators, relatives or employers later verified fragments of the account. They could confirm
times, damaged equipment, missing records or physical traces, but not the sequence that connected
them. The official report reduced the event to stress, malfunction, trespassing or an unexplained
absence.

The surviving version of the doll that collected people's eyes continues to be repeated because it
leaves one practical warning. When an impossible detail repeats under controlled conditions, it is
no longer harmless simply because it has not attacked yet. The most disturbing possibility is not
that the danger survived Daniel's escape. It is that the escape taught it a quieter way to follow
the next person.
