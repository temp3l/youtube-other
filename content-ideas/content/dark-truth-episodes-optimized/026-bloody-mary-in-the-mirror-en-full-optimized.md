# Narration Script

Mia said the name three times as a joke. Her reflection opened its eyes before she did.

Mia Carter returned to her former boarding school for a reunion weekend. With two old friends, she entered a closed washroom where students had once dared one another to perform the Bloody Mary ritual.

At first, the detail seemed small enough to dismiss. They lit one candle and repeated the name. Nothing happened until Mia noticed her reflection breathing out of rhythm. The setting offered no obvious sign of danger, which made the warning easy to treat as superstition rather than procedure. The character paused long enough to test the obvious explanation, but the environment answered before reassurance could settle in. A familiar detail was now wrong in a way that could be checked rather than merely felt.

That explanation lasted only until the next night. Every mirror in the washroom began showing a different memory from Mia's school years, including events she had tried not to remember. The repeated detail mattered because it returned at a precise moment, not as a vague memory after the event. The reaction was practical rather than reckless: check the exit, verify the time, preserve a recording and avoid touching what had changed. None of those precautions restored the earlier version of events.

From that point, the pattern stopped looking accidental. A friend touched the glass and disappeared. Her handprint remained on the inside surface. Instead of escalating immediately, the threat waited for a human response and then adapted to it. Silence followed for several seconds. That pause mattered because it gave everyone present time to understand the same thing independently: the incident had responded to them.

The first real warning came when the ordinary rules failed. The reflected woman claimed she did not kill people. She replaced the version of them they were ashamed to acknowledge. The sound motif returned—a bathroom drip, vibrating glass, and whispered counting—but now it came from a place where no ordinary source could be found. No dramatic attack followed immediately. Instead, the setting became less dependable—distance, sound, reflections or locked boundaries no longer behaved as expected.

What followed changed the meaning of everything before it. Mia discovered that every mirror in the school opened onto the same dark room. Trapped students stood behind the glass, repeating gestures a moment too late. This was the point at which observation replaced disbelief. The evidence did not explain the event; it only proved that the earlier incidents belonged to the same design. The evidence created a worse problem than disbelief. Once the detail had been confirmed, every ordinary decision carried the risk of satisfying a rule nobody fully understood.

The danger then became personal. Breaking the mirror multiplied the problem. Each fragment showed a different captive and a different version of Mia. The rule was narrow enough to offer hope, but cruel enough that one emotional mistake could break it. The safest choice was also the hardest one emotionally. The threat had learned which voice, memory or promise could make caution feel cruel.

A reasonable attempt to escape made the situation worse. The entity copied Mia's memories but made small errors. It knew public events, not private sensory details. The attempted solution did not erase the danger. It revealed what the danger had been waiting for. They acted quickly, but the result showed that speed alone was useless. The danger was not chasing their bodies; it was steering their decisions.

For a few minutes, the plan appeared to work. Mia forced it to repeat the past aloud, correcting every false detail and admitting the truth she had hidden from her friends. The temporary silence created the most dangerous moment, because it encouraged the survivors to believe the worst had passed. For a moment, ordinary background noise returned and the space looked normal again. That false calm allowed the next change to arrive without warning.

The apparent ending did not survive until morning. The missing friend returned when Mia refused to surrender the shame the entity used as a doorway. What remained could be photographed or recorded, but not interpreted in a way that made the survivors feel safe. Later investigators could confirm isolated details, but not the chain connecting them. The survivor was left with proof strong enough to remember and incomplete enough to doubt.

The final piece of evidence arrived later. At home, Mia covered every mirror except one. That mirror no longer reflected her room. It showed the abandoned school washroom with someone counting toward three. That final detail transformed survival from an ending into another stage of the pattern. Nothing in the final record explained how the pattern continued. It only showed that the survivor's method of escape had become part of the threat's next approach.

Before the final decision, the surviving record describes a deliberate verification attempt. Times were compared, exits were checked and the source of a bathroom drip, vibrating glass, and whispered counting was traced as far as the setting allowed. Each check eliminated one ordinary explanation but also consumed time. The characters were not waiting passively for another scare; they were learning that the phenomenon became more precise whenever they tried to measure it. What first appeared to be atmosphere had become a signalling system.

A second escape attempt failed for a different reason. The physical route remained open, but one familiar detail encouraged the wrong choice: a trusted voice, a believable message or an object placed exactly where safety should have been. The hesitation that followed gave the threat time to reposition itself. By the time the deception was recognised, returning to the previous location was no longer possible.
