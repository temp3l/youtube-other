# Narration Script

Two children stood outside Noah's motel room in freezing rain. They did not want shelter. They wanted permission.

Noah Price was a paramedic driving home after a long transfer through the mountains. Ice closed the main road shortly after midnight, forcing him into a roadside motel with six rooms, one flickering sign and a manager who slid the key across the counter without asking for identification.

At first, the detail seemed small enough to dismiss. The manager gave Noah Room 4 and one strange instruction. After midnight, he was not to invite anyone inside, even if they sounded injured. Noah assumed the warning concerned thieves until the manager added, 'They cannot enter unless you help them.' The setting offered no obvious sign of danger, which made the warning easy to treat as superstition rather than procedure. The character paused long enough to test the obvious explanation, but the environment answered before reassurance could settle in. A familiar detail was now wrong in a way that could be checked rather than merely felt.

That explanation lasted only until the next night. At 12:17, two soft knocks came from the door. A boy of perhaps twelve stood outside with a younger girl. Both wore thin clothes despite the freezing rain. Their faces were lowered, and neither appeared to shiver. The boy asked to use Noah's phone because their mother was waiting in a car. The repeated detail mattered because it returned at a precise moment, not as a vague memory after the event. The reaction was practical rather than reckless: check the exit, verify the time, preserve a recording and avoid touching what had changed. None of those precautions restored the earlier version of events.

From that point, the pattern stopped looking accidental. Noah kept the security chain fastened. He offered to call someone for them. The boy replied that a call would not help. They needed to come inside. Each time Noah suggested another solution, the child repeated the request with exactly the same rhythm, as though reading from a script. Instead of escalating immediately, the threat waited for a human response and then adapted to it. Silence followed for several seconds. That pause mattered because it gave everyone present time to understand the same thing independently: the incident had responded to them.

The first real warning came when the ordinary rules failed. The girl began describing Noah's final emergency call of the evening. She repeated the patient's address, the medication Noah had administered and the last sentence the dying man had spoken. Those details had never been broadcast. The boy said the patient was sitting in their car and wanted Noah to finish what he had started. The sound motif returned—freezing rain, two soft knocks, and a child speaking through a closed door—but now it came from a place where no ordinary source could be found. No dramatic attack followed immediately. Instead, the setting became less dependable—distance, sound, reflections or locked boundaries no longer behaved as expected.

What followed changed the meaning of everything before it. When Noah looked through the gap, the boy raised his face. His eyes were completely black, with no white, colour or reflection. The hallway light shone on his wet skin but vanished inside those eyes. Noah slammed the door and heard two knocks again, now from the bathroom window. This was the point at which observation replaced disbelief. The evidence did not explain the event; it only proved that the earlier incidents belonged to the same design. The evidence created a worse problem than disbelief. Once the detail had been confirmed, every ordinary decision carried the risk of satisfying a rule nobody fully understood.

The danger then became personal. The room began to shrink. The walls moved inward by centimetres, and the television showed the inside of a car travelling through the storm. Noah saw himself behind the wheel. In the back seat sat the two children, both watching him through the screen. The rule was narrow enough to offer hope, but cruel enough that one emotional mistake could break it. The safest choice was also the hardest one emotionally. The threat had learned which voice, memory or promise could make caution feel cruel.

A reasonable attempt to escape made the situation worse. Emergency dispatch answered his call, but the dispatcher used Noah's own voice. It reported that two children had died on that road eleven years earlier after a motorist refused to let them into his vehicle. The report then gave the motorist's name: Noah Price. The attempted solution did not erase the danger. It revealed what the danger had been waiting for. They acted quickly, but the result showed that speed alone was useless. The danger was not chasing their bodies; it was steering their decisions.

For a few minutes, the plan appeared to work. Noah understood that every compassionate phrase he had used—'I can help,' 'wait there,' 'let me see'—had counted as partial permission. He stood in the centre of the room, stated his full name and permanent address, and declared that the room belonged only to him until sunrise. The walls stopped moving. The temporary silence created the most dangerous moment, because it encouraged the survivors to believe the worst had passed. For a moment, ordinary background noise returned and the space looked normal again. That false calm allowed the next change to arrive without warning.

The apparent ending did not survive until morning. The children became angry for the first time. They no longer sounded young. They recited the names of patients Noah had failed to save and promised that opening the door would give him one chance to correct every death. Noah placed his hands over his ears and repeated that no invitation had been given. What remained could be photographed or recorded, but not interpreted in a way that made the survivors feel safe. Later investigators could confirm isolated details, but not the chain connecting them. The survivor was left with proof strong enough to remember and incomplete enough to doubt.

The final piece of evidence arrived later. At dawn, the rain stopped. The hallway was empty, but two wet footprints ended on the carpet inside his room. The motel manager had disappeared, and county records showed that the building had been abandoned for nine years. That final detail transformed survival from an ending into another stage of the pattern. Nothing in the final record explained how the pattern continued. It only showed that the survivor's method of escape had become part of the threat's next approach.

Noah later found one final audio file on his phone. It contained two knocks, followed by his own voice saying, 'Come in.' He did not remember speaking. Since then, he has never opened a door after midnight without first checking that his reflection still has eyes.
