# Narration Script

The emergency broadcast cut in without authorisation. Instead of a weather warning, it displayed
Mara's name and the time 4:12 a.m.

Mara Evans was a overnight broadcast engineer when the account began in a local television station
during a violent electrical storm. The object at the centre of it was an unauthorised emergency
transmission from a decommissioned tower. It did not arrive with violence, a body or a clear threat.
It entered the story through routine work, which gave Mara every reason to remain calm. Later, that
ordinary beginning became important because the earliest details were the only ones that never
changed.

The warning was already there: The station handbook contained a taped-over note: if the presenter
looks away from the camera, evacuate without speaking. Mara did what most careful people would do.
The instruction was photographed, logged and treated as a precaution left by someone superstitious.
Nothing in the room appeared damaged. No alarm had triggered. The doors, windows and locks were all
in the condition described by the records.

The signal listed local addresses seconds before power failed at each one. Mara repeated the test
rather than trusting a first impression. Equipment was restarted, measurements were taken again and
timestamps were compared. The result remained the same. That repetition was more disturbing than a
sudden shock because it removed the easiest explanation: simple error.

For a while, the incident could still be contained. Mara stepped away, returned later and asked
another person to observe without being told what to expect. That observer noticed a different
detail, but it pointed in the same direction. The account was no longer based on one frightened
person's memory.

A colourless presenter appeared on screen and described accidents before they happened, always
ending with the words, 'The engineer remains on duty.' The episode's recurring sounds—a flat test
tone, broadcast static, and mechanical relay clicks—began appearing only at the moment the pattern
advanced. They stayed low in the background, never announcing exactly what had changed. Their
purpose was to make the audience recognise danger before the characters did.

The pattern followed one rule: Anything broadcast before 4:12 became true. Cutting the feed merely
moved it to another screen. Once Mara understood that rule, the earlier incidents stopped looking
random. They formed a sequence: observation, response, escalation and correction. The threat was
testing how a reasonable person reacted and changing its behaviour after every failed attempt to
control it.

Mara began an investigation rather than waiting for another incident. Old records, archived
messages, previous owners and maintenance reports were compared. The dates were separated by years,
sometimes decades, but the language was almost identical. Different witnesses had described the same
timing, the same repeated sound and the same moment when a harmless anomaly became personal.

The records also revealed what earlier victims had done wrong. Some tried to destroy the object
immediately. Others shared it, moved away or convinced themselves the pattern had ended. None of
those actions broke the rule. They only changed where the next incident occurred.

Mara tried the safest option first. The source was isolated, the location was secured and an outside
contact was told exactly where to look if communication stopped. For several minutes, the measure
appeared successful. Then the recurring sound returned from a place it could not physically reach.

Archived tapes showed identical transmissions before a flood, a factory fire and a bridge collapse.
Every tape ended with the engineer's chair empty. This was the first piece of evidence that survived
outside the protagonist's perception. It could be photographed, copied or compared. It did not prove
a supernatural explanation, but it proved the official explanation was incomplete.

The discovery also changed the emotional stakes. Until then, Mara had been protecting a job, a
reputation or a piece of evidence. Now the threat had reached into personal history and selected
details designed to produce hesitation. Leaving was still possible, but leaving someone or something
behind was no longer simple.

The other staff evacuated, yet studio cameras continued following invisible movement through
deserted rooms. Mara responded with another practical measure: stopping the work, separating people
from the source and calling for help. The response failed in a specific way. The failure revealed
that distance alone would not end the pattern.

At this point, the account accelerated. Familiar exits became unreliable. Ordinary background noise
disappeared between incidents. Small details shifted position whenever nobody was watching. The
location seemed less like a haunted place and more like a mechanism closing around one person.

The only workable plan was this: Mara routed the signal into a closed internal loop and replaced the
live output with prerecorded weather footage. It relied on observation instead of physical strength.
The goal was not to destroy something that did not obey ordinary limits. The goal was to force it
into repeating the one behaviour it could not change.

Before acting, Mara tested the plan on a smaller scale. The result created a brief opening and
confirmed the rule, but it also warned the threat. From that moment forward, every sound and
movement arrived faster. The entity no longer waited to be noticed.

The presenter changed scripts and began narrating Mara's movements in real time. When Mara reached
for the master switch, the screen showed her doing it first—and showed the ceiling collapsing. She
stopped, moved the opposite way, and used the delay to disconnect the old transmitter physically.
Mara had only one opportunity to commit to the plan. Reversing course would have repeated the
earlier mistake and strengthened the trap. The final action worked not because it was powerful, but
because it contradicted what the threat expected a frightened person to do.

For several seconds, the location became completely silent. The recurring motif stopped. Lights,
clocks or electronic displays returned to normal. Mara did not interpret that silence as victory. It
felt more like the moment after a machine had finished recording.

At exactly 4:12, every monitor went black and the storm stopped. Mara survived, but one frame in
every programme aired by the station now shows her sitting alone in the control room, staring at
something behind the camera.

In the hours after the escape, Mara documented everything while the sequence was still fresh. The
notes were duplicated, timestamps were preserved and physical evidence was stored separately. By
morning, several details had already changed. A name was missing from one copy. A time differed by
one minute. A photograph showed an object in the wrong position.

Investigators, relatives or employers later verified fragments of the account. They could confirm
times, damaged equipment, missing records or physical traces, but not the sequence that connected
them. The official report reduced the event to stress, malfunction, trespassing or an unexplained
absence.

The surviving version of the emergency broadcast named the next victim continues to be repeated
because it leaves one practical warning. When an impossible detail repeats under controlled
conditions, it is no longer harmless simply because it has not attacked yet. The most disturbing
possibility is not that the danger survived Mara's escape. It is that the escape taught it a quieter
way to follow the next person.
