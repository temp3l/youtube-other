# Narration Script

Mia was told never to clean a room marked with a black ribbon. At 3:10 in the morning, the ribbon appeared on her own apartment door.

Mia Alvarez accepted the overnight housekeeping job because the hotel paid more than the restaurants near her apartment. The building rose forty floors above the city, but after midnight it felt abandoned. Whole corridors remained dark to save electricity, and the elevators sometimes opened on floors no guest had selected.

At first, the detail seemed small enough to dismiss. On her first shift, the supervisor introduced an elderly cleaner named Mara. Mara pushed an old steel cart that squeaked on one wheel and carried no room list. Before they separated, she gave Mia one rule: if a black ribbon was tied around a door handle, Mia was not to knock, enter, or remove it. Mara would clean that room alone. The setting offered no obvious sign of danger, which made the warning easy to treat as superstition rather than procedure. The character paused long enough to test the obvious explanation, but the environment answered before reassurance could settle in. A familiar detail was now wrong in a way that could be checked rather than merely felt.

That explanation lasted only until the next night. At dawn, an ambulance arrived for the guest in 1812. The man had died in his sleep. The next night, a ribbon appeared on 904. Its occupant slipped in the bathroom before breakfast. Management called both deaths unrelated, but Mia remembered seeing Mara's cart outside each room hours before anyone raised an alarm. The repeated detail mattered because it returned at a precise moment, not as a vague memory after the event. The reaction was practical rather than reckless: check the exit, verify the time, preserve a recording and avoid touching what had changed. None of those precautions restored the earlier version of events.

From that point, the pattern stopped looking accidental. Mia confronted Mara in the service lift. Mara did not deny the pattern. She said she never caused a death. She cleaned what death left behind before the living were forced to see it. When the elevator bell sounded at the sixth floor, Mara grabbed the cart and whispered, 'Tonight, do not follow me.' Instead of escalating immediately, the threat waited for a human response and then adapted to it. Silence followed for several seconds. That pause mattered because it gave everyone present time to understand the same thing independently: the incident had responded to them.

The first real warning came when the ordinary rules failed. Mia followed her to Room 614. The room was still occupied by a businessman speaking on the phone, yet dark stains were already spreading across the carpet beneath him. A cracked watch lay near the bed, stopped at 4:07. The man's watch was still on his wrist. When he laughed at something on the call, the stain widened toward his feet. The sound motif returned—housekeeping cart wheels, a spray-bottle hiss, and a single hotel elevator bell—but now it came from a place where no ordinary source could be found. No dramatic attack followed immediately. Instead, the setting became less dependable—distance, sound, reflections or locked boundaries no longer behaved as expected.

What followed changed the meaning of everything before it. Inside the bathroom cabinet, Mia found objects that did not belong to the guest: a child's inhaler, a wedding ring, a scorched passport, and her own apartment key. Every item carried a paper label with a date and room number. Her key was marked for 3:10 a.m. on Christmas morning. This was the point at which observation replaced disbelief. The evidence did not explain the event; it only proved that the earlier incidents belonged to the same design. The evidence created a worse problem than disbelief. Once the detail had been confirmed, every ordinary decision carried the risk of satisfying a rule nobody fully understood.

The danger then became personal. Mara finally explained that the hotel was not choosing victims. It was a waiting room built over a place where unfinished deaths gathered. The black ribbon marked the next open threshold. If the ribbon was removed, the death did not disappear. It searched for another door. The rule was narrow enough to offer hope, but cruel enough that one emotional mistake could break it. The safest choice was also the hardest one emotionally. The threat had learned which voice, memory or promise could make caution feel cruel.

A reasonable attempt to escape made the situation worse. Mia stole the ribbon from her personnel file and tied it to an empty conference room. For several minutes, nothing happened. Then the ballroom filled with guests who had died in the hotel over the previous century. They stood silently in wet clothes, hospital gowns, burned uniforms and evening dresses, all facing the false door. The attempted solution did not erase the danger. It revealed what the danger had been waiting for. They acted quickly, but the result showed that speed alone was useless. The danger was not chasing their bodies; it was steering their decisions.

For a few minutes, the plan appeared to work. The elevator bell rang once. Every dead guest turned toward Mia. The conference-room walls began to pulse with the hiss of a spray bottle. Mara pushed her cart between Mia and the crowd and offered the threshold the only thing it had not recorded: her own name. The dead stopped moving. Every staff photograph containing Mara faded blank. The temporary silence created the most dangerous moment, because it encouraged the survivors to believe the worst had passed. For a moment, ordinary background noise returned and the space looked normal again. That false calm allowed the next change to arrive without warning.

The apparent ending did not survive until morning. By sunrise, no employee remembered Mara. Mia alone remembered the woman because she still held the key from Room 614. Management promoted her to senior night cleaner and placed Mara's steel cart in her storage bay. A new room list waited on top. What remained could be photographed or recorded, but not interpreted in a way that made the survivors feel safe. Later investigators could confirm isolated details, but not the chain connecting them. The survivor was left with proof strong enough to remember and incomplete enough to doubt.

The final piece of evidence arrived later. The first assignment was not inside the hotel. It listed Mia's home address, the time 3:10 a.m., and the name of the person registered to watch the recording of her statement. Beneath the paper lay a black ribbon, freshly tied. That final detail transformed survival from an ending into another stage of the pattern. Nothing in the final record explained how the pattern continued. It only showed that the survivor's method of escape had become part of the threat's next approach.

Mia never explained whether she continued cleaning to protect people or because the hotel had made refusal impossible. The only reliable warning is the one Mara gave her: if a black ribbon appears on your door, do not remove it. And if you hear cart wheels outside, do not look through the peephole.
