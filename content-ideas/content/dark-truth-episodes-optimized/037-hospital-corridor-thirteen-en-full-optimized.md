# Narration Script

The lift opened onto Corridor Thirteen. The hospital had only twelve floors.

Paul Dean was a biomedical technician when the account began in a city hospital during overnight
maintenance. The object at the centre of it was a maintenance alert from a nonexistent ward. It did
not arrive with violence, a body or a clear threat. It entered the story through routine work, which
gave Paul every reason to remain calm. Later, that ordinary beginning became important because the
earliest details were the only ones that never changed.

The warning was already there: A handwritten label inside the lift panel read: if Floor 13 appears,
do not let any monitor identify you. Paul did what most careful people would do. The instruction was
photographed, logged and treated as a precaution left by someone superstitious. Nothing in the room
appeared damaged. No alarm had triggered. The doors, windows and locks were all in the condition
described by the records.

Paul followed the alert and found occupied rooms with curtains closed but no visible patients. Paul
repeated the test rather than trusting a first impression. Equipment was restarted, measurements
were taken again and timestamps were compared. The result remained the same. That repetition was
more disturbing than a sudden shock because it removed the easiest explanation: simple error.

For a while, the incident could still be contained. Paul stepped away, returned later and asked
another person to observe without being told what to expect. That observer noticed a different
detail, but it pointed in the same direction. The account was no longer based on one frightened
person's memory.

Charts displayed admission dates from the future and diagnoses written in Paul's own handwriting.
The episode's recurring sounds—a patient monitor beep, trolley wheels, and a lift chime with no
floor announcement—began appearing only at the moment the pattern advanced. They stayed low in the
background, never announcing exactly what had changed. Their purpose was to make the audience
recognise danger before the characters did.

The pattern followed one rule: Once a monitor matched a visitor's pulse, the corridor began
rewriting that person as a patient. Once Paul understood that rule, the earlier incidents stopped
looking random. They formed a sequence: observation, response, escalation and correction. The threat
was testing how a reasonable person reacted and changing its behaviour after every failed attempt to
control it.

Paul began an investigation rather than waiting for another incident. Old records, archived
messages, previous owners and maintenance reports were compared. The dates were separated by years,
sometimes decades, but the language was almost identical. Different witnesses had described the same
timing, the same repeated sound and the same moment when a harmless anomaly became personal.

The records also revealed what earlier victims had done wrong. Some tried to destroy the object
immediately. Others shared it, moved away or convinced themselves the pattern had ended. None of
those actions broke the rule. They only changed where the next incident occurred.

Paul tried the safest option first. The source was isolated, the location was secured and an outside
contact was told exactly where to look if communication stopped. For several minutes, the measure
appeared successful. Then the recurring sound returned from a place it could not physically reach.

An empty bed displayed Paul's heart rhythm before he touched any sensor. This was the first piece of
evidence that survived outside the protagonist's perception. It could be photographed, copied or
compared. It did not prove a supernatural explanation, but it proved the official explanation was
incomplete.

The discovery also changed the emotional stakes. Until then, Paul had been protecting a job, a
reputation or a piece of evidence. Now the threat had reached into personal history and selected
details designed to produce hesitation. Leaving was still possible, but leaving someone or something
behind was no longer simple.

Staff on the corridor insisted Paul had been unconscious there for weeks. Security footage showed
him arriving on a trolley the previous night. Paul responded with another practical measure:
stopping the work, separating people from the source and calling for help. The response failed in a
specific way. The failure revealed that distance alone would not end the pattern.

At this point, the account accelerated. Familiar exits became unreliable. Ordinary background noise
disappeared between incidents. Small details shifted position whenever nobody was watching. The
location seemed less like a haunted place and more like a mechanism closing around one person.

The only workable plan was this: Paul disconnected the central telemetry bridge so the ward could no
longer maintain a consistent identity for him. It relied on observation instead of physical
strength. The goal was not to destroy something that did not obey ordinary limits. The goal was to
force it into repeating the one behaviour it could not change.

Before acting, Paul tested the plan on a smaller scale. The result created a brief opening and
confirmed the rule, but it also warned the threat. From that moment forward, every sound and
movement arrived faster. The entity no longer waited to be noticed.

Every room door opened. Inside each was Paul at a different age, suffering a different possible
death. The lift would not respond, so he forced it between floors and climbed into the shaft while
the monitors synchronised into one continuous tone. Paul had only one opportunity to commit to the
plan. Reversing course would have repeated the earlier mistake and strengthened the trap. The final
action worked not because it was powerful, but because it contradicted what the threat expected a
frightened person to do.

For several seconds, the location became completely silent. The recurring motif stopped. Lights,
clocks or electronic displays returned to normal. Paul did not interpret that silence as victory. It
felt more like the moment after a machine had finished recording.

He returned to Floor Twelve before 3:18. His employee badge still worked, but his personnel file had
been replaced by a discharge summary. The diagnosis read: 'Patient continues to believe he escaped.'

In the hours after the escape, Paul documented everything while the sequence was still fresh. The
notes were duplicated, timestamps were preserved and physical evidence was stored separately. By
morning, several details had already changed. A name was missing from one copy. A time differed by
one minute. A photograph showed an object in the wrong position.

Investigators, relatives or employers later verified fragments of the account. They could confirm
times, damaged equipment, missing records or physical traces, but not the sequence that connected
them. The official report reduced the event to stress, malfunction, trespassing or an unexplained
absence.

The surviving version of the hospital corridor that shouldn't exist continues to be repeated because
it leaves one practical warning. When an impossible detail repeats under controlled conditions, it
is no longer harmless simply because it has not attacked yet. The most disturbing possibility is not
that the danger survived Paul's escape. It is that the escape taught it a quieter way to follow the
next person.
