# Narration Script

Elena raised her hand. Her reflection lowered its own and pointed behind her.

Elena Marks was a graphic designer when the account began in a modern apartment lined with mirrored
wardrobe doors. The object at the centre of it was a reflection that moved one second too early. It
did not arrive with violence, a body or a clear threat. It entered the story through routine work,
which gave Elena every reason to remain calm. Later, that ordinary beginning became important
because the earliest details were the only ones that never changed.

The warning was already there: A message appeared backward in condensation: DO NOT LET HER FINISH
YOUR MOVEMENT. Elena did what most careful people would do. The instruction was photographed, logged
and treated as a precaution left by someone superstitious. Nothing in the room appeared damaged. No
alarm had triggered. The doors, windows and locks were all in the condition described by the
records.

During a video call, Elena's reflection smiled before Elena did. Elena repeated the test rather than
trusting a first impression. Equipment was restarted, measurements were taken again and timestamps
were compared. The result remained the same. That repetition was more disturbing than a sudden shock
because it removed the easiest explanation: simple error.

For a while, the incident could still be contained. Elena stepped away, returned later and asked
another person to observe without being told what to expect. That observer noticed a different
detail, but it pointed in the same direction. The account was no longer based on one frightened
person's memory.

It began performing actions Elena had not decided to make, then waited for her to copy them. The
episode's recurring sounds—a low glass hum, fingertips dragging on mirror, and reversed
whispers—began appearing only at the moment the pattern advanced. They stayed low in the background,
never announcing exactly what had changed. Their purpose was to make the audience recognise danger
before the characters did.

The pattern followed one rule: Every action Elena repeated after the reflection made it transferred
a small part of her life to the other side. Once Elena understood that rule, the earlier incidents
stopped looking random. They formed a sequence: observation, response, escalation and correction.
The threat was testing how a reasonable person reacted and changing its behaviour after every failed
attempt to control it.

Elena began an investigation rather than waiting for another incident. Old records, archived
messages, previous owners and maintenance reports were compared. The dates were separated by years,
sometimes decades, but the language was almost identical. Different witnesses had described the same
timing, the same repeated sound and the same moment when a harmless anomaly became personal.

The records also revealed what earlier victims had done wrong. Some tried to destroy the object
immediately. Others shared it, moved away or convinced themselves the pattern had ended. None of
those actions broke the rule. They only changed where the next incident occurred.

Elena tried the safest option first. The source was isolated, the location was secured and an
outside contact was told exactly where to look if communication stopped. For several minutes, the
measure appeared successful. Then the recurring sound returned from a place it could not physically
reach.

Dark laptop screens showed a second apartment where another Elena lived with altered photographs and
reversed writing. This was the first piece of evidence that survived outside the protagonist's
perception. It could be photographed, copied or compared. It did not prove a supernatural
explanation, but it proved the official explanation was incomplete.

The discovery also changed the emotional stakes. Until then, Elena had been protecting a job, a
reputation or a piece of evidence. Now the threat had reached into personal history and selected
details designed to produce hesitation. Leaving was still possible, but leaving someone or something
behind was no longer simple.

Elena found childhood memories missing after copying the reflection's gestures. Her mother
remembered a hospital stay Elena could not recall. Elena responded with another practical measure:
stopping the work, separating people from the source and calling for help. The response failed in a
specific way. The failure revealed that distance alone would not end the pattern.

At this point, the account accelerated. Familiar exits became unreliable. Ordinary background noise
disappeared between incidents. Small details shifted position whenever nobody was watching. The
location seemed less like a haunted place and more like a mechanism closing around one person.

The only workable plan was this: She covered every reflective surface and tested the other Elena
with private scars, habits and memories. It relied on observation instead of physical strength. The
goal was not to destroy something that did not obey ordinary limits. The goal was to force it into
repeating the one behaviour it could not change.

Before acting, Elena tested the plan on a smaller scale. The result created a brief opening and
confirmed the rule, but it also warned the threat. From that moment forward, every sound and
movement arrived faster. The entity no longer waited to be noticed.

The reflection displayed the same scar on the wrong side, then cut its palm. A matching wound opened
on Elena's hand. Elena refused to mirror the next movement and instead smashed the wardrobe while
the other version was halfway through stepping forward. Elena had only one opportunity to commit to
the plan. Reversing course would have repeated the earlier mistake and strengthened the trap. The
final action worked not because it was powerful, but because it contradicted what the threat
expected a frightened person to do.

For several seconds, the location became completely silent. The recurring motif stopped. Lights,
clocks or electronic displays returned to normal. Elena did not interpret that silence as victory.
It felt more like the moment after a machine had finished recording.

The glass fell outward, not inward. Elena woke alone among the fragments. Her friends said she
seemed normal, except cameras no longer captured her image. In every photograph, a second apartment
appeared behind the people she loved.

In the hours after the escape, Elena documented everything while the sequence was still fresh. The
notes were duplicated, timestamps were preserved and physical evidence was stored separately. By
morning, several details had already changed. A name was missing from one copy. A time differed by
one minute. A photograph showed an object in the wrong position.

Investigators, relatives or employers later verified fragments of the account. They could confirm
times, damaged equipment, missing records or physical traces, but not the sequence that connected
them. The official report reduced the event to stress, malfunction, trespassing or an unexplained
absence.

The surviving version of her reflection stopped copying her continues to be repeated because it
leaves one practical warning. When an impossible detail repeats under controlled conditions, it is
no longer harmless simply because it has not attacked yet. The most disturbing possibility is not
that the danger survived Elena's escape. It is that the escape taught it a quieter way to follow the
next person.
