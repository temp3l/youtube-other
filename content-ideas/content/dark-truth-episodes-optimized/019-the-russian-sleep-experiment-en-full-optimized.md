# Narration Script

The experiment promised freedom if five prisoners stayed awake for thirty days. On day nine, they stopped asking to be released.

The Russian Sleep Experiment is an internet horror story, not a documented historical event. This version treats it openly as fiction and follows Dr. Anya Volkov, a physician assigned to a Cold War research bunker where five prisoners were exposed to an experimental stimulant gas.

At first, the detail seemed small enough to dismiss. For the first days, the men talked, exercised and argued about what they would do after release. Cameras watched through reinforced windows. Microphones carried every conversation to the control room, while a ventilation fan kept the colourless gas moving through the chamber. The setting offered no obvious sign of danger, which made the warning easy to treat as superstition rather than procedure. The character paused long enough to test the obvious explanation, but the environment answered before reassurance could settle in. A familiar detail was now wrong in a way that could be checked rather than merely felt.

That explanation lasted only until the next night. On the fifth day, the prisoners became secretive. They whispered into vents and covered the observation windows with pages torn from books. On the ninth, one man screamed until his voice failed. The other four did not approach him or ask for help. The repeated detail mattered because it returned at a precise moment, not as a vague memory after the event. The reaction was practical rather than reckless: check the exit, verify the time, preserve a recording and avoid touching what had changed. None of those precautions restored the earlier version of events.

From that point, the pattern stopped looking accidental. Microphones later captured the prisoners confessing crimes they could not have known. They described classified executions, private memories of researchers and events that had not yet happened. Each confession ended with the same sentence: 'You are still asleep.' Instead of escalating immediately, the threat waited for a human response and then adapted to it. Silence followed for several seconds. That pause mattered because it gave everyone present time to understand the same thing independently: the incident had responded to them.

The first real warning came when the ordinary rules failed. Volkov reviewed the neurological data and found that the gas was not simply preventing sleep. It was suppressing the brain's ability to wake from a shared dream state. The prisoners were conscious, but part of each mind remained trapped in the same nightmare. The sound motif returned—a ventilation fan, microphone static, and a medical monitor—but now it came from a place where no ordinary source could be found. No dramatic attack followed immediately. Instead, the setting became less dependable—distance, sound, reflections or locked boundaries no longer behaved as expected.

What followed changed the meaning of everything before it. When oxygen levels fell, the military supervisor ordered the chamber opened. The prisoners begged for the gas to remain. They claimed that full wakefulness would release whatever had been moving between their dreams. This was the point at which observation replaced disbelief. The evidence did not explain the event; it only proved that the earlier incidents belonged to the same design. The evidence created a worse problem than disbelief. Once the detail had been confirmed, every ordinary decision carried the risk of satisfying a rule nobody fully understood.

The danger then became personal. Medical teams entered. The chamber was damaged, but Volkov found none of the impossible mutilations later associated with the legend. The horror was quieter: the prisoners reacted to people and objects no one else could see, and their monitor readings briefly synchronised into one heartbeat. The rule was narrow enough to offer hope, but cruel enough that one emotional mistake could break it. The safest choice was also the hardest one emotionally. The threat had learned which voice, memory or promise could make caution feel cruel.

A reasonable attempt to escape made the situation worse. The supervisor ordered the gas restored and the surviving subjects returned. Volkov refused. She shut down ventilation, entered the chamber and administered sedatives, believing ordinary sleep might separate the shared nightmare. The attempted solution did not erase the danger. It revealed what the danger had been waiting for. They acted quickly, but the result showed that speed alone was useless. The danger was not chasing their bodies; it was steering their decisions.

For a few minutes, the plan appeared to work. Four prisoners died as their brain activity fell. Their faces relaxed for the first time in days. The final subject remained awake despite enough sedative to stop his breathing. He told Volkov that the prisoners had never been the test subjects. The temporary silence created the most dangerous moment, because it encouraged the survivors to believe the worst had passed. For a moment, ordinary background noise returned and the space looked normal again. That false calm allowed the next change to arrive without warning.

The apparent ending did not survive until morning. The monitor screens changed from five patient files to the names of everyone in the observation room. According to the final subject, the bunker itself was the dream, and the experiment was measuring how long the researchers could avoid waking. What remained could be photographed or recorded, but not interpreted in a way that made the survivors feel safe. Later investigators could confirm isolated details, but not the chain connecting them. The survivor was left with proof strong enough to remember and incomplete enough to doubt.

The final piece of evidence arrived later. Volkov cut power to the facility. In the darkness, the ventilation fan continued turning. When emergency lights returned, the chamber was empty. Every clock displayed day one. That final detail transformed survival from an ending into another stage of the pattern. Nothing in the final record explained how the pattern continued. It only showed that the survivor's method of escape had become part of the threat's next approach.

Before the final decision, the surviving record describes a deliberate verification attempt. Times were compared, exits were checked and the source of a ventilation fan, microphone static, and a medical monitor was traced as far as the setting allowed. Each check eliminated one ordinary explanation but also consumed time. The characters were not waiting passively for another scare; they were learning that the phenomenon became more precise whenever they tried to measure it. What first appeared to be atmosphere had become a signalling system.

The recovered report ended with a line typed under Volkov's signature: 'If you are reading this, the trial is still running.' That sentence is fictional, like the entire experiment. Its power comes from making the reader wonder why a story known to be false can still feel like a memory.
