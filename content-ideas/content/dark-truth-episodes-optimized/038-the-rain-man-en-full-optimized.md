# Narration Script

Every photograph of the flood showed the same man. He stood dry beneath the rain, always facing
Laura's house.

Laura Finch was a local journalist when the account began in a coastal town during weeks of
relentless rain. The object at the centre of it was a silent man in a long coat who appeared before
disappearances. It did not arrive with violence, a body or a clear threat. It entered the story
through routine work, which gave Laura every reason to remain calm. Later, that ordinary beginning
became important because the earliest details were the only ones that never changed.

The warning was already there: Laura's mother begged her not to publish his picture and said, 'He
notices families who remember him.' Laura did what most careful people would do. The instruction was
photographed, logged and treated as a precaution left by someone superstitious. Nothing in the room
appeared damaged. No alarm had triggered. The doors, windows and locks were all in the condition
described by the records.

Witnesses described different faces but the same coat and the same impossible dryness. Laura
repeated the test rather than trusting a first impression. Equipment was restarted, measurements
were taken again and timestamps were compared. The result remained the same. That repetition was
more disturbing than a sudden shock because it removed the easiest explanation: simple error.

For a while, the incident could still be contained. Laura stepped away, returned later and asked
another person to observe without being told what to expect. That observer noticed a different
detail, but it pointed in the same direction. The account was no longer based on one frightened
person's memory.

Laura found him in childhood photographs taken during the storm that killed her father. The
episode's recurring sounds—heavy rain, gutter water, and wet footsteps indoors—began appearing only
at the moment the pattern advanced. They stayed low in the background, never announcing exactly what
had changed. Their purpose was to make the audience recognise danger before the characters did.

The pattern followed one rule: The man came to collect a life promised during an earlier rescue.
Secrecy delayed him, but every new storm renewed the debt. Once Laura understood that rule, the
earlier incidents stopped looking random. They formed a sequence: observation, response, escalation
and correction. The threat was testing how a reasonable person reacted and changing its behaviour
after every failed attempt to control it.

Laura began an investigation rather than waiting for another incident. Old records, archived
messages, previous owners and maintenance reports were compared. The dates were separated by years,
sometimes decades, but the language was almost identical. Different witnesses had described the same
timing, the same repeated sound and the same moment when a harmless anomaly became personal.

The records also revealed what earlier victims had done wrong. Some tried to destroy the object
immediately. Others shared it, moved away or convinced themselves the pattern had ended. None of
those actions broke the rule. They only changed where the next incident occurred.

Laura tried the safest option first. The source was isolated, the location was secured and an
outside contact was told exactly where to look if communication stopped. For several minutes, the
measure appeared successful. Then the recurring sound returned from a place it could not physically
reach.

Wet footprints began at Laura's front door and ended beneath sealed floorboards. This was the first
piece of evidence that survived outside the protagonist's perception. It could be photographed,
copied or compared. It did not prove a supernatural explanation, but it proved the official
explanation was incomplete.

The discovery also changed the emotional stakes. Until then, Laura had been protecting a job, a
reputation or a piece of evidence. Now the threat had reached into personal history and selected
details designed to produce hesitation. Leaving was still possible, but leaving someone or something
behind was no longer simple.

Her mother admitted that when Laura nearly drowned as a child, the family had invited the man inside
and offered 'someone else' in exchange. Laura responded with another practical measure: stopping the
work, separating people from the source and calling for help. The response failed in a specific way.
The failure revealed that distance alone would not end the pattern.

At this point, the account accelerated. Familiar exits became unreliable. Ordinary background noise
disappeared between incidents. Small details shifted position whenever nobody was watching. The
location seemed less like a haunted place and more like a mechanism closing around one person.

The only workable plan was this: Laura broadcast the photographs, dates and bargain live, believing
public knowledge would break the private contract. It relied on observation instead of physical
strength. The goal was not to destroy something that did not obey ordinary limits. The goal was to
force it into repeating the one behaviour it could not change.

Before acting, Laura tested the plan on a smaller scale. The result created a brief opening and
confirmed the rule, but it also warned the threat. From that moment forward, every sound and
movement arrived faster. The entity no longer waited to be noticed.

The storm stopped outside, but rain began falling inside the television studio. The man stepped
through the live weather screen, dry as ever. Laura named herself as the original beneficiary and
refused to name another victim. Laura had only one opportunity to commit to the plan. Reversing
course would have repeated the earlier mistake and strengthened the trap. The final action worked
not because it was powerful, but because it contradicted what the threat expected a frightened
person to do.

For several seconds, the location became completely silent. The recurring motif stopped. Lights,
clocks or electronic displays returned to normal. Laura did not interpret that silence as victory.
It felt more like the moment after a machine had finished recording.

The broadcast ended in static. Laura vanished, but no one else disappeared that night. Since then,
every regional weather graphic has contained a tiny figure beneath the rain icon, facing the viewer.

In the hours after the escape, Laura documented everything while the sequence was still fresh. The
notes were duplicated, timestamps were preserved and physical evidence was stored separately. By
morning, several details had already changed. A name was missing from one copy. A time differed by
one minute. A photograph showed an object in the wrong position.

Investigators, relatives or employers later verified fragments of the account. They could confirm
times, damaged equipment, missing records or physical traces, but not the sequence that connected
them. The official report reduced the event to stress, malfunction, trespassing or an unexplained
absence.

The surviving version of the rain man only appeared during storms continues to be repeated because
it leaves one practical warning. When an impossible detail repeats under controlled conditions, it
is no longer harmless simply because it has not attacked yet. The most disturbing possibility is not
that the danger survived Laura's escape. It is that the escape taught it a quieter way to follow the
next person.
