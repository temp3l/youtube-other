# Narration Script

The man danced beneath a streetlight with a smile too wide for his face. When Elena crossed the road, he folded sideways and followed.

Elena Brooks was a nurse walking home after a late shift. The streets were almost empty, and dawn was still an hour away when she heard hard shoes tapping in an irregular rhythm behind her.

At first, the detail seemed small enough to dismiss. Several blocks away, a thin man moved beneath a streetlight in exaggerated dance steps. His coat was too large, his arms hung loosely, and his smile remained fixed even when the rest of his face changed direction. The setting offered no obvious sign of danger, which made the warning easy to treat as superstition rather than procedure. The character paused long enough to test the obvious explanation, but the environment answered before reassurance could settle in. A familiar detail was now wrong in a way that could be checked rather than merely felt.

That explanation lasted only until the next night. Elena crossed the road. The man stopped, tilted his head and crossed without taking a normal step. His body leaned sideways until it seemed ready to fall, then glided to the opposite pavement while his shoes continued tapping. The repeated detail mattered because it returned at a precise moment, not as a vague memory after the event. The reaction was practical rather than reckless: check the exit, verify the time, preserve a recording and avoid touching what had changed. None of those precautions restored the earlier version of events.

From that point, the pattern stopped looking accidental. Each time Elena changed direction, he appeared ahead without passing her. The streetlights between them went dark one by one. Under every remaining light, he stood closer and smiled more broadly. Instead of escalating immediately, the threat waited for a human response and then adapted to it. Silence followed for several seconds. That pause mattered because it gave everyone present time to understand the same thing independently: the incident had responded to them.

The first real warning came when the ordinary rules failed. She entered an all-night shop and asked the clerk to call police. The clerk saw no one outside. Security footage showed Elena reacting to empty pavement, but the automatic doors opened repeatedly as though someone were pacing across the sensor. The sound motif returned—hard shoe taps, distant traffic, and fluorescent streetlights—but now it came from a place where no ordinary source could be found. No dramatic attack followed immediately. Instead, the setting became less dependable—distance, sound, reflections or locked boundaries no longer behaved as expected.

What followed changed the meaning of everything before it. The smiling man began copying Elena's movements several seconds before she made them. When she raised one hand, he had already raised his. When she prepared to run, he leaned forward first. She realised he was not imitating her. He was predicting her. This was the point at which observation replaced disbelief. The evidence did not explain the event; it only proved that the earlier incidents belonged to the same design. The evidence created a worse problem than disbelief. Once the detail had been confirmed, every ordinary decision carried the risk of satisfying a rule nobody fully understood.

The danger then became personal. Elena used that prediction against him. She looked toward an alley and shifted her weight as though she would sprint, then stepped backward into a taxi stopping at the kerb. The man launched into the alley and vanished beyond the light. The rule was narrow enough to offer hope, but cruel enough that one emotional mistake could break it. The safest choice was also the hardest one emotionally. The threat had learned which voice, memory or promise could make caution feel cruel.

A reasonable attempt to escape made the situation worse. The driver locked the doors and pulled away. Elena explained what had happened. The driver said nothing. In the rear-view mirror, the smiling man was seated beside her, though the seat felt empty when Elena touched it. The attempted solution did not erase the danger. It revealed what the danger had been waiting for. They acted quickly, but the result showed that speed alone was useless. The danger was not chasing their bodies; it was steering their decisions.

For a few minutes, the plan appeared to work. The taxi passed Elena's apartment three times. The driver insisted they had not reached it. Outside, every street sign carried Elena's name. The smiling reflection lifted one finger to its lips. The temporary silence created the most dangerous moment, because it encouraged the survivors to believe the worst had passed. For a moment, ordinary background noise returned and the space looked normal again. That false calm allowed the next change to arrive without warning.

The apparent ending did not survive until morning. Elena forced the door open at a traffic light and ran into a police station. She remained beneath fluorescent lights until sunrise. Officers found no taxi matching her description and no man on nearby cameras. What remained could be photographed or recorded, but not interpreted in a way that made the survivors feel safe. Later investigators could confirm isolated details, but not the chain connecting them. The survivor was left with proof strong enough to remember and incomplete enough to doubt.

The final piece of evidence arrived later. At home, Elena discovered hard shoe marks across the ceiling above her bed. The marks ended directly over the pillow. Her phone's step counter recorded another person walking beside her for the entire route. That final detail transformed survival from an ending into another stage of the pattern. Nothing in the final record explained how the pattern continued. It only showed that the survivor's method of escape had become part of the threat's next approach.

Before the final decision, the surviving record describes a deliberate verification attempt. Times were compared, exits were checked and the source of hard shoe taps, distant traffic, and fluorescent streetlights was traced as far as the setting allowed. Each check eliminated one ordinary explanation but also consumed time. The characters were not waiting passively for another scare; they were learning that the phenomenon became more precise whenever they tried to measure it. What first appeared to be atmosphere had become a signalling system.

A second escape attempt failed for a different reason. The physical route remained open, but one familiar detail encouraged the wrong choice: a trusted voice, a believable message or an object placed exactly where safety should have been. The hesitation that followed gave the threat time to reposition itself. By the time the deception was recognised, returning to the previous location was no longer possible. The only remaining plan depended on the rule revealed by the earlier evidence.

Elena still works nights, but she no longer walks home. Sometimes her patients smile before waking from sedation. When that happens, she hears hard shoes tapping somewhere in the corridor, always one step ahead of her own.
