# Narration Script

Mara's train passed her stop and continued for twenty minutes without slowing. Then it arrived at Kisaragi Station—a place missing from every map.

Mara Lewis was an exchange student travelling home after a late shift. The commuter train was usually crowded, but shortly before midnight she looked up from her phone and realised every other passenger had gone.

At first, the detail seemed small enough to dismiss. The train passed three familiar stations without slowing. Outside, the city lights gave way to dark fields even though the line never left the metropolitan area. An announcement played through the speakers, but the station name contained no clear consonants. The setting offered no obvious sign of danger, which made the warning easy to treat as superstition rather than procedure. The character paused long enough to test the obvious explanation, but the environment answered before reassurance could settle in. A familiar detail was now wrong in a way that could be checked rather than merely felt.

That explanation lasted only until the next night. The doors eventually opened onto a narrow platform. A sign read KISARAGI. Mara searched the name online and found no railway listing, map entry or recent photograph. Messages from strangers advised her to remain aboard. She stepped out to inspect the sign, and the doors closed behind her. The repeated detail mattered because it returned at a precise moment, not as a vague memory after the event. The reaction was practical rather than reckless: check the exit, verify the time, preserve a recording and avoid touching what had changed. None of those precautions restored the earlier version of events.

From that point, the pattern stopped looking accidental. The station clock moved backward. No staff answered the intercom. Beyond the platform, distant festival drums sounded from a tunnel, although the surrounding area appeared empty. Each time Mara posted an update, replies appeared before her message had finished uploading. Instead of escalating immediately, the threat waited for a human response and then adapted to it. Silence followed for several seconds. That pause mattered because it gave everyone present time to understand the same thing independently: the incident had responded to them.

The first real warning came when the ordinary rules failed. A one-legged man emerged on the tracks. He called for Mara to move away from the tunnel, then vanished when a train horn sounded. The horn came from behind her, but the rails vibrated in front of her. The sound motif returned—train rails, a distorted announcement, and distant festival drums—but now it came from a place where no ordinary source could be found. No dramatic attack followed immediately. Instead, the setting became less dependable—distance, sound, reflections or locked boundaries no longer behaved as expected.

What followed changed the meaning of everything before it. Mara followed a road away from the station and accepted help from a driver in an old car. He spoke politely and promised to take her to the nearest town. In the mirror, his reflection turned the wheel several seconds after his hands did. This was the point at which observation replaced disbelief. The evidence did not explain the event; it only proved that the earlier incidents belonged to the same design. The evidence created a worse problem than disbelief. Once the detail had been confirmed, every ordinary decision carried the risk of satisfying a rule nobody fully understood.

The danger then became personal. Her phone received a message from her own account: GET OUT BEFORE HE REACHES THE MOUNTAIN. Mara opened the door while the car slowed at a crossing and rolled into the grass. The driver continued without reacting, his reflection still watching her. The rule was narrow enough to offer hope, but cruel enough that one emotional mistake could break it. The safest choice was also the hardest one emotionally. The threat had learned which voice, memory or promise could make caution feel cruel.

A reasonable attempt to escape made the situation worse. The distorted station announcement returned from a dark tunnel ahead. Mara followed it, believing the sound would lead to the platform. Inside, the walls were covered with names and dates scratched into the concrete. Several names belonged to people replying to her online thread. The attempted solution did not erase the danger. It revealed what the danger had been waiting for. They acted quickly, but the result showed that speed alone was useless. The danger was not chasing their bodies; it was steering their decisions.

For a few minutes, the plan appeared to work. A train arrived without headlights. Every passenger looked like Mara at a different age. A child version pressed both hands to the glass. An elderly version shook her head. The doors opened, and the announcement finally became clear: 'Passengers may return only by leaving someone behind.' The temporary silence created the most dangerous moment, because it encouraged the survivors to believe the worst had passed. For a moment, ordinary background noise returned and the space looked normal again. That false calm allowed the next change to arrive without warning.

The apparent ending did not survive until morning. Mara refused to board. She walked along the track toward the loudest drums until the tunnel lights went out. When they returned, she was standing at her normal station at 5:02 in the morning. Security footage showed no train arriving. What remained could be photographed or recorded, but not interpreted in a way that made the survivors feel safe. Later investigators could confirm isolated details, but not the chain connecting them. The survivor was left with proof strong enough to remember and incomplete enough to doubt.

The final piece of evidence arrived later. Her online posts remained visible, but the timestamps covered nine years rather than one night. The earliest reply had been written by Mara herself, using an account she had not yet created. That final detail transformed survival from an ending into another stage of the pattern. Nothing in the final record explained how the pattern continued. It only showed that the survivor's method of escape had become part of the threat's next approach.

Before the final decision, the surviving record describes a deliberate verification attempt. Times were compared, exits were checked and the source of train rails, a distorted announcement, and distant festival drums was traced as far as the setting allowed. Each check eliminated one ordinary explanation but also consumed time. The characters were not waiting passively for another scare; they were learning that the phenomenon became more precise whenever they tried to measure it. What first appeared to be atmosphere had become a signalling system.

Mara stopped using trains after midnight. Months later, a station announcement played through her apartment intercom. It used her voice and said that the final passenger was still waiting at Kisaragi Station.
