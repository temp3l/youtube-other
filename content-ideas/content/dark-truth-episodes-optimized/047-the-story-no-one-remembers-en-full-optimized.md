# Narration Script

Everyone remembered that the story terrified them. Nobody could remember what happened in it.

Tom Avery was a folklore researcher when the account began in a coastal library. The object at the
centre of it was a handwritten manuscript with missing pages. It did not arrive with violence, a
body or a clear threat. It entered the story through routine work, which gave Tom every reason to
remain calm. Later, that ordinary beginning became important because the earliest details were the
only ones that never changed.

The warning was already there: The catalogue card said: DO NOT READ A NAME ALOUD TWICE. Tom did what
most careful people would do. The instruction was photographed, logged and treated as a precaution
left by someone superstitious. Nothing in the room appeared damaged. No alarm had triggered. The
doors, windows and locks were all in the condition described by the records.

The handwriting changed between paragraphs, as though different readers had continued the same
account. Tom repeated the test rather than trusting a first impression. Equipment was restarted,
measurements were taken again and timestamps were compared. The result remained the same. That
repetition was more disturbing than a sudden shock because it removed the easiest explanation:
simple error.

For a while, the incident could still be contained. Tom stepped away, returned later and asked
another person to observe without being told what to expect. That observer noticed a different
detail, but it pointed in the same direction. The account was no longer based on one frightened
person's memory.

People named in the text disappeared from photographs, databases and memories. The episode's
recurring sounds—a library clock, turning pages, and whispered words fading out—began appearing only
at the moment the pattern advanced. They stayed low in the background, never announcing exactly what
had changed. Their purpose was to make the audience recognise danger before the characters did.

The pattern followed one rule: The story preserved itself by replacing real people with characters,
then erasing anyone who could describe the plot. Once Tom understood that rule, the earlier
incidents stopped looking random. They formed a sequence: observation, response, escalation and
correction. The threat was testing how a reasonable person reacted and changing its behaviour after
every failed attempt to control it.

Tom began an investigation rather than waiting for another incident. Old records, archived messages,
previous owners and maintenance reports were compared. The dates were separated by years, sometimes
decades, but the language was almost identical. Different witnesses had described the same timing,
the same repeated sound and the same moment when a harmless anomaly became personal.

The records also revealed what earlier victims had done wrong. Some tried to destroy the object
immediately. Others shared it, moved away or convinced themselves the pattern had ended. None of
those actions broke the rule. They only changed where the next incident occurred.

Tom tried the safest option first. The source was isolated, the location was secured and an outside
contact was told exactly where to look if communication stopped. For several minutes, the measure
appeared successful. Then the recurring sound returned from a place it could not physically reach.

Tom's audio recording contained silence wherever the manuscript mentioned him. This was the first
piece of evidence that survived outside the protagonist's perception. It could be photographed,
copied or compared. It did not prove a supernatural explanation, but it proved the official
explanation was incomplete.

The discovery also changed the emotional stakes. Until then, Tom had been protecting a job, a
reputation or a piece of evidence. Now the threat had reached into personal history and selected
details designed to produce hesitation. Leaving was still possible, but leaving someone or something
behind was no longer simple.

The final page predicted the order in which Tom's friends would forget him. Tom responded with
another practical measure: stopping the work, separating people from the source and calling for
help. The response failed in a specific way. The failure revealed that distance alone would not end
the pattern.

At this point, the account accelerated. Familiar exits became unreliable. Ordinary background noise
disappeared between incidents. Small details shifted position whenever nobody was watching. The
location seemed less like a haunted place and more like a mechanism closing around one person.

The only workable plan was this: He removed his name from every copy and replaced it with a
fictional character. It relied on observation instead of physical strength. The goal was not to
destroy something that did not obey ordinary limits. The goal was to force it into repeating the one
behaviour it could not change.

Before acting, Tom tested the plan on a smaller scale. The result created a brief opening and
confirmed the rule, but it also warned the threat. From that moment forward, every sound and
movement arrived faster. The entity no longer waited to be noticed.

The catalogue immediately listed the fictional character as the author. Tom's colleagues began
discussing him as someone from a book. He wrote a new ending in which the story forgot its own
title, forcing the manuscript to erase its index and opening page. Tom had only one opportunity to
commit to the plan. Reversing course would have repeated the earlier mistake and strengthened the
trap. The final action worked not because it was powerful, but because it contradicted what the
threat expected a frightened person to do.

For several seconds, the location became completely silent. The recurring motif stopped. Lights,
clocks or electronic displays returned to normal. Tom did not interpret that silence as victory. It
felt more like the moment after a machine had finished recording.

Tom survived, but friends remembered him only as a vague character. The manuscript later gained a
new first line: 'Tom Avery first heard about this from you.' Beneath it was the viewer's current
date.

In the hours after the escape, Tom documented everything while the sequence was still fresh. The
notes were duplicated, timestamps were preserved and physical evidence was stored separately. By
morning, several details had already changed. A name was missing from one copy. A time differed by
one minute. A photograph showed an object in the wrong position.

Investigators, relatives or employers later verified fragments of the account. They could confirm
times, damaged equipment, missing records or physical traces, but not the sequence that connected
them. The official report reduced the event to stress, malfunction, trespassing or an unexplained
absence.

The surviving version of the story that erases everyone who hears it continues to be repeated
because it leaves one practical warning. When an impossible detail repeats under controlled
conditions, it is no longer harmless simply because it has not attacked yet. The most disturbing
possibility is not that the danger survived Tom's escape. It is that the escape taught it a quieter
way to follow the next person.
