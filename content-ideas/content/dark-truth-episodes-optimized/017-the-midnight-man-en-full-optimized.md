# Narration Script

The ritual required Daniel to carry a candle until 3:33 a.m. The first time it went out, something whispered from inside his shadow.

Daniel Cross built an audience by livestreaming rituals he did not believe. For the Midnight Man challenge, he rented an abandoned country house and invited two friends, Leah and Marcus, to help operate cameras.

At first, the detail seemed small enough to dismiss. Before midnight, each participant wrote a full name on paper, added a drop of blood and placed the paper before the front door. They lit candles, knocked twenty-two times and opened the door on the final knock. Nothing stood outside. The setting offered no obvious sign of danger, which made the warning easy to treat as superstition rather than procedure. The character paused long enough to test the obvious explanation, but the environment answered before reassurance could settle in. A familiar detail was now wrong in a way that could be checked rather than merely felt.

That explanation lasted only until the next night. At exactly midnight, every candle flame turned blue. The front door closed by itself. Daniel laughed for the livestream until the chat began describing a tall figure behind him. The camera showed only a dark vertical shape where Daniel's shadow should have been. The repeated detail mattered because it returned at a precise moment, not as a vague memory after the event. The reaction was practical rather than reckless: check the exit, verify the time, preserve a recording and avoid touching what had changed. None of those precautions restored the earlier version of events.

From that point, the pattern stopped looking accidental. The rules required each person to keep moving and relight a candle within ten seconds if it went out. When Marcus's flame died, the corridor changed. The kitchen door led upstairs, and every window reflected a room that did not exist. Instead of escalating immediately, the threat waited for a human response and then adapted to it. Silence followed for several seconds. That pause mattered because it gave everyone present time to understand the same thing independently: the incident had responded to them.

The first real warning came when the ordinary rules failed. Leah broke the salt-circle rule after hearing her dead sister call from the second floor. Daniel followed and found a bedroom reconstructed from Leah's childhood. The voice knew the final argument Leah had never told anyone about. The sound motif returned—matches striking, twenty-two knocks, and a candle flame fluttering—but now it came from a place where no ordinary source could be found. No dramatic attack followed immediately. Instead, the setting became less dependable—distance, sound, reflections or locked boundaries no longer behaved as expected.

What followed changed the meaning of everything before it. The livestream chat filled with messages from accounts belonging to missing people. They warned Daniel not to trust the clock. Several viewers said the same figure was now standing inside their own rooms, just beyond the camera frame. This was the point at which observation replaced disbelief. The evidence did not explain the event; it only proved that the earlier incidents belonged to the same design. The evidence created a worse problem than disbelief. Once the detail had been confirmed, every ordinary decision carried the risk of satisfying a rule nobody fully understood.

The danger then became personal. The Midnight Man never appeared directly. Instead, the house built rooms from each person's worst memory and forced them to choose a door. Marcus entered a hospital room containing his father. Leah entered her sister's bedroom. Neither returned. The rule was narrow enough to offer hope, but cruel enough that one emotional mistake could break it. The safest choice was also the hardest one emotionally. The threat had learned which voice, memory or promise could make caution feel cruel.

A reasonable attempt to escape made the situation worse. Daniel realised the ritual did not summon the entity. It gave fear a name, a time and an invitation. Every viewer repeating the rules created another doorway. He ended the stream, but the viewer count continued rising. The attempted solution did not erase the danger. It revealed what the danger had been waiting for. They acted quickly, but the result showed that speed alone was useless. The danger was not chasing their bodies; it was steering their decisions.

For a few minutes, the plan appeared to work. At 3:33, Daniel's candle remained lit. He reached the front door and expected the ritual to end. The clock changed to 3:32. Each time the minute passed, it reset. The house had no intention of allowing the appointed time to arrive. The temporary silence created the most dangerous moment, because it encouraged the survivors to believe the worst had passed. For a moment, ordinary background noise returned and the space looked normal again. That false calm allowed the next change to arrive without warning.

The apparent ending did not survive until morning. Daniel burned the papers bearing their names. The flames moved backward into the match, restoring each page except his. He then spoke his name incorrectly, denying the ritual the identity it had been given. The front door opened. What remained could be photographed or recorded, but not interpreted in a way that made the survivors feel safe. Later investigators could confirm isolated details, but not the chain connecting them. The survivor was left with proof strong enough to remember and incomplete enough to doubt.

The final piece of evidence arrived later. Police found Daniel alone at sunrise. The house contained no upper floor, no blood and no sign of Leah or Marcus. The livestream archive lasted three hours, but its timestamp showed that it had begun the following night. That final detail transformed survival from an ending into another stage of the pattern. Nothing in the final record explained how the pattern continued. It only showed that the survivor's method of escape had become part of the threat's next approach.

Before the final decision, the surviving record describes a deliberate verification attempt. Times were compared, exits were checked and the source of matches striking, twenty-two knocks, and a candle flame fluttering was traced as far as the setting allowed. Each check eliminated one ordinary explanation but also consumed time. The characters were not waiting passively for another scare; they were learning that the phenomenon became more precise whenever they tried to measure it. What first appeared to be atmosphere had become a signalling system.

Daniel never streamed again. At 3:32 each morning, however, his phone receives a notification that the broadcast is about to begin. The viewer count is always two higher than it was the night before.
