# Narration Script

Elena believed the dog beneath her bed was calming her. By morning, the dog was dead in the
hallway—and someone had written a message on the mirror.

Elena Ward was a university student house-sitting for her aunt when the story began in a rain-
soaked suburban house on the edge of Brighton. The first reports did not describe an obvious
monster or violent attack. They described a wet tongue brushing the back of her hand from
beneath the bed. That detail mattered because it returned at moments when no ordinary source
could be found.

Most frightening stories become exaggerated after they are repeated. This one became more
precise. Dates, rooms, recordings and witness statements differed in minor ways, but the
central sequence remained the same. A warning was ignored, an impossible pattern appeared, and
the person at the centre of it survived with evidence that made survival difficult to
celebrate.

The first event seemed harmless enough to rationalise. Her aunt leaves for a medical conference
and asks Elena to care for Bramble, an old golden retriever who always sleeps beside the bed.
The setting was described in enough detail to make the account testable: the time, weather and
nearby objects were all recorded. Later, those ordinary details became important because
several of them changed without explanation.

The next incident removed some of that comfort. During a storm, the power fails. Elena hears a
cupboard open downstairs, but police tell her an alarm fault probably caused it. A second
witness or recording appeared to support part of the account, but it also introduced a
contradiction. That contradiction prevented the event from being dismissed as fatigue,
coincidence or a simple technical fault.

By then, the pattern was deliberate. Each time she reaches below the bed, something licks her
hand. She assumes Bramble is frightened by thunder. The protagonist tried a reasonable safety
measure rather than moving deeper into danger. The measure failed in a specific way, showing
that the threat could anticipate normal human responses.

The central rule was broken shortly afterward. At 2:40 a.m., Elena hears slow dripping from the
bathroom and finds Bramble hanging lifeless in the shower, water and blood ticking into the
tub. From this point onward, each decision carried a visible consequence. The recurring sound
or object returned whenever the protagonist moved closer to understanding the rule.

The discovery changed the meaning of everything that came before. On the steamed mirror, a
finger has written: HUMANS CAN LICK TOO. The discovery connected the present incident to
earlier victims or earlier versions of the same legend. It also revealed that the familiar
warning had been misunderstood.

There was no perfect solution, only a narrow opportunity. Elena hides in a wardrobe while an
intruder walks through the bedroom, imitating the dog's breathing and scratching the floor with
his nails. The escape plan relied on observation rather than physical strength. One small
inconsistency offered the only way to tell the genuine path from the trap.

The threat recognised the plan almost immediately. She triggers the car alarm remotely. A
neighbour comes outside, forcing the intruder to flee through the loft hatch. The threat
reacted by using a trusted voice, memory or familiar place. That made the final choice
emotional as well as practical.

For several minutes, escape appeared possible. Police find a nest in the attic containing food
wrappers, photographs of Elena asleep, and a notebook recording every night she visited the
house. The immediate danger ended without a complete explanation. Authorities, relatives or
witnesses could verify only fragments of what had happened.

The official account ended there. The final page says: SHE REACHED DOWN FIRST. The last piece
of evidence changed the meaning of the apparent escape. It suggested that the event had
continued after the protagonist believed it was over.

The account is still repeated because it leaves one question unresolved. Did Elena Ward defeat
the thing at the centre of the story, or did the encounter simply teach it how to remain
hidden? The final warning is therefore simple: when the same impossible detail appears twice,
do not wait for a third occurrence to prove that it is real.
