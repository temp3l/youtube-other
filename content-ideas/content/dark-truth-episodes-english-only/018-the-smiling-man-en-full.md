# Narration Script

The man danced beneath a streetlight with a smile too wide for his face. When Elena crossed the
road, he folded sideways and followed.

Elena Brooks was a nurse walking home after a late shift when the story began in empty city
streets shortly before dawn. The first reports did not describe an obvious monster or violent
attack. They described hard shoe taps in an irregular dancing rhythm. That detail mattered
because it returned at moments when no ordinary source could be found.

Most frightening stories become exaggerated after they are repeated. This one became more
precise. Dates, rooms, recordings and witness statements differed in minor ways, but the
central sequence remained the same. A warning was ignored, an impossible pattern appeared, and
the person at the centre of it survived with evidence that made survival difficult to
celebrate.

The first event seemed harmless enough to rationalise. Elena sees a thin man moving in
exaggerated dance steps several blocks away. The setting was described in enough detail to make
the account testable: the time, weather and nearby objects were all recorded. Later, those
ordinary details became important because several of them changed without explanation.

The next incident removed some of that comfort. He repeatedly stops under streetlights, tilts
his head and smiles without blinking. A second witness or recording appeared to support part of
the account, but it also introduced a contradiction. That contradiction prevented the event
from being dismissed as fatigue, coincidence or a simple technical fault.

By then, the pattern was deliberate. When Elena changes direction, he appears ahead without
passing her. The protagonist tried a reasonable safety measure rather than moving deeper into
danger. The measure failed in a specific way, showing that the threat could anticipate normal
human responses.

The central rule was broken shortly afterward. She enters an all-night shop, but the clerk
cannot see the man through the glass. From this point onward, each decision carried a visible
consequence. The recurring sound or object returned whenever the protagonist moved closer to
understanding the rule.

The discovery changed the meaning of everything that came before. Security footage shows Elena
alone, reacting to empty pavement. The discovery connected the present incident to earlier
victims or earlier versions of the same legend. It also revealed that the familiar warning had
been misunderstood.

There was no perfect solution, only a narrow opportunity. The smiling figure begins mimicking
her movements several seconds before she makes them. The escape plan relied on observation
rather than physical strength. One small inconsistency offered the only way to tell the genuine
path from the trap.

The threat recognised the plan almost immediately. Elena uses the prediction against him by
pretending to run, then moving backward into a taxi. The threat reacted by using a trusted
voice, memory or familiar place. That made the final choice emotional as well as practical.

For several minutes, escape appeared possible. The driver takes her home, but his rear-view
mirror shows the smiling man seated beside her. The immediate danger ended without a complete
explanation. Authorities, relatives or witnesses could verify only fragments of what had
happened.

The official account ended there. Elena reaches a police station and survives until sunrise.
The last piece of evidence changed the meaning of the apparent escape. It suggested that the
event had continued after the protagonist believed it was over.

One final detail was discovered later. For weeks afterward, strangers in crowds begin smiling a
moment before she turns toward them. No reliable account explains what happened next. The
surviving evidence supports more than one interpretation, and none of them is comforting.

The account is still repeated because it leaves one question unresolved. Did Elena Brooks
defeat the thing at the centre of the story, or did the encounter simply teach it how to remain
hidden? The final warning is therefore simple: when the same impossible detail appears twice,
do not wait for a third occurrence to prove that it is real.
