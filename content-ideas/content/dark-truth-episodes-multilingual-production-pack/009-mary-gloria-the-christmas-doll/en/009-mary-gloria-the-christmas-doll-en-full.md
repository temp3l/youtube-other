# Narration Script

The doll arrived without a sender. Its card said, 'For the daughter who should have been mine.'

Adrian Cole was a widowed father raising his eight-year-old daughter when the story began in a
snowbound family home on Christmas Eve. The first reports did not describe an obvious monster
or violent attack. They described a music-box version of a Christmas carol playing one note too
slowly. That detail mattered because it returned at moments when no ordinary source could be
found.

Most frightening stories become exaggerated after they are repeated. This one became more
precise. Dates, rooms, recordings and witness statements differed in minor ways, but the
central sequence remained the same. A warning was ignored, an impossible pattern appeared, and
the person at the centre of it survived with evidence that made survival difficult to
celebrate.

The first event seemed harmless enough to rationalise. Adrian's daughter Lily receives an
antique porcelain doll named Mary Gloria. The setting was described in enough detail to make
the account testable: the time, weather and nearby objects were all recorded. Later, those
ordinary details became important because several of them changed without explanation.

The next incident removed some of that comfort. The doll speaks only when adults leave the room
and asks Lily to call it Mother. A second witness or recording appeared to support part of the
account, but it also introduced a contradiction. That contradiction prevented the event from
being dismissed as fatigue, coincidence or a simple technical fault.

By then, the pattern was deliberate. Family photographs begin changing, placing the doll where
Lily's late mother once stood. The protagonist tried a reasonable safety measure rather than
moving deeper into danger. The measure failed in a specific way, showing that the threat could
anticipate normal human responses.

The central rule was broken shortly afterward. At midnight, every wrapped present contains a
piece of the same red dress. From this point onward, each decision carried a visible
consequence. The recurring sound or object returned whenever the protagonist moved closer to
understanding the rule.

The discovery changed the meaning of everything that came before. Adrian finds shipping labels
showing the doll has been sent between grieving families for almost a century. The discovery
connected the present incident to earlier victims or earlier versions of the same legend. It
also revealed that the familiar warning had been misunderstood.

There was no perfect solution, only a narrow opportunity. Each family lost a child shortly
after accepting Mary Gloria into the home. The escape plan relied on observation rather than
physical strength. One small inconsistency offered the only way to tell the genuine path from
the trap.

The threat recognised the plan almost immediately. The doll offers Adrian a bargain: his wife
can return if Lily gives up her place. The threat reacted by using a trusted voice, memory or
familiar place. That made the final choice emotional as well as practical.

For several minutes, escape appeared possible. Adrian burns the doll, but its porcelain face
appears beneath the icing of the Christmas cake. The immediate danger ended without a complete
explanation. Authorities, relatives or witnesses could verify only fragments of what had
happened.

The official account ended there. Lily rejects the bargain by naming every real memory she has
of her mother. The last piece of evidence changed the meaning of the apparent escape. It
suggested that the event had continued after the protagonist believed it was over.

One final detail was discovered later. At dawn, the doll is gone, but a child's voice answers
from inside the chimney: 'Next Christmas, then.' No reliable account explains what happened
next. The surviving evidence supports more than one interpretation, and none of them is
comforting.

The account is still repeated because it leaves one question unresolved. Did Adrian Cole defeat
the thing at the centre of the story, or did the encounter simply teach it how to remain
hidden? The final warning is therefore simple: when the same impossible detail appears twice,
do not wait for a third occurrence to prove that it is real.
