# Kisaragi Station — Full YouTube Horror Story

Mara Lewis knew she had missed her stop before the train announcement confirmed it.

The doors stayed closed. The carriage lights dimmed, brightened, and dimmed again. Outside the window, familiar apartment blocks had given way to black fields and telephone poles that seemed to repeat in the same order.

Mara checked the route map above the door. Her station was still glowing behind them.

Twenty minutes passed without the train slowing.

Then the speakers crackled.

The announcement contained no recognisable words. It sounded like a human voice with every consonant removed—a wet sequence of vowels stretched into a sentence. The other passengers did not react.

Mara looked around.

There were no other passengers.

The train stopped at a narrow platform beneath three weak lamps. A sign outside read:

KISARAGI.

Mara searched the name. No station appeared. No railway line used it. She posted a message online, expecting someone to tell her she had misread the sign.

The first reply arrived almost immediately.

Do not leave the train.

The doors opened.

Beyond them stood an empty platform, a locked waiting room, and a clock showing 12:43.

Mara stayed seated until she heard knocking beneath the carriage floor.

Three slow knocks.

Then three more, directly below her feet.

She stepped onto the platform.

The doors closed behind her.

The train moved away without making a sound.

Her phone still had signal. Replies flooded in. People asked for photographs. Some warned her not to enter the tunnel at the far end of the platform. One user claimed to remember an old post from a woman who had reached the same station years earlier and never returned.

Mara photographed the clock.

It now showed 12:41.

Two minutes earlier.

From somewhere beyond the tracks came the low beat of a drum.

She called the railway emergency number. The operator asked her to repeat the station name, then placed her on hold. While Mara waited, footsteps scraped along the rails.

A man was walking toward her from the darkness.

At first she thought he was injured. Then he entered the light and she saw that he had only one leg. He moved without crutches, rising and dropping in a smooth rhythm as though an invisible wire supported him.

He raised one hand.

“Do not stand near the tracks,” he said.

A train horn screamed from inside the tunnel.

Mara covered her ears.

When she looked again, the man was gone.

Online, a new message appeared from her own account.

DO NOT TRUST THE ROAD.

Mara had not typed it.

She ran past the station building and found a narrow road descending through the trees. Behind her, the station speakers repeated the vowel-only announcement.

A car approached.

The driver was a middle-aged man with a calm voice. He said he knew the station and offered to take her to the nearest town.

Mara hesitated.

Then she saw movement in his side mirror.

The man’s reflection was still looking forward, but the real driver had turned toward her.

Mara stepped back.

The driver smiled.

His reflection did not.

She ran.

The car followed at walking speed. No matter how quickly Mara moved, its headlights remained the same distance behind her. Messages arrived from friends asking questions she had not sent yet.

Are you in the tunnel?

Did the driver touch you?

Why did you get into the car?

The road ended at a concrete tunnel beneath the mountain.

From inside came the sound of a station announcement.

This time it spoke clearly.

“Mara Lewis. The next train is waiting.”

The car stopped behind her. The driver opened his door.

Mara noticed something the online warnings had missed: every false path at Kisaragi was quiet. The train that had carried her there made no rail noise. The car made no engine noise. The one-legged man had vanished when a real train horn sounded.

The station imitated voices and shapes.

But it could not perfectly imitate sound.

Mara entered the tunnel and followed the loudest noise she could hear: metal wheels shrieking against rails.

A train burst from the darkness.

Its headlights were on.

Mara flattened herself against the wall as it passed. The final carriage door stood open.

Inside, every seat was occupied.

Every passenger had Mara’s face.

A child version of her pressed both hands against the glass. An elderly Mara sat beside her. Teenagers, adults and women with injuries Mara had never suffered watched silently as the train slowed.

The open door aligned with her.

The passengers smiled.

Mara stepped backward.

The driver’s voice came from behind her.

“That is the real train.”

But his words arrived half a second before his mouth moved.

Mara turned away from the open door and followed the fading wheel noise deeper into the tunnel.

The lights went out.

When she opened her eyes, she was sitting on a morning commuter train in her own city.

Passengers surrounded her. Her phone showed dozens of messages posted during the night.

Their timestamps covered nine years.

The police found no station named Kisaragi. The railway company said Mara’s train had arrived on schedule and that security footage showed her asleep in her seat.

For several months, nothing else happened.

Then, one evening, the intercom in Mara’s apartment crackled.

A voice without consonants whispered from the speaker.

The display read:

NEXT STOP: KISARAGI.

Mara never opened the door.

But from the hallway came three slow knocks.

Then three more, directly beneath her feet.
