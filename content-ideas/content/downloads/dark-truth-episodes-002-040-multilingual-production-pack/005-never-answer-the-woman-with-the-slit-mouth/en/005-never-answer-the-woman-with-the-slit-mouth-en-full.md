# Narration Script

A masked woman asked Paul whether she was beautiful. Every answer he gave changed the street
around him.

Paul Reeves was an English teacher walking home after the last train when the story began in a
quiet commuter district outside Tokyo. The first reports did not describe an obvious monster or
violent attack. They described the metallic click of scissors opening in an empty street. That
detail mattered because it returned at moments when no ordinary source could be found.

Most frightening stories become exaggerated after they are repeated. This one became more
precise. Dates, rooms, recordings and witness statements differed in minor ways, but the
central sequence remained the same. A warning was ignored, an impossible pattern appeared, and
the person at the centre of it survived with evidence that made survival difficult to
celebrate.

The first event seemed harmless enough to rationalise. Paul hears students discussing a woman
who appears near closed stations and asks strangers a question. The setting was described in
enough detail to make the account testable: the time, weather and nearby objects were all
recorded. Later, those ordinary details became important because several of them changed
without explanation.

The next incident removed some of that comfort. After missing the last bus, he encounters a
tall woman in a beige coat and surgical mask. A second witness or recording appeared to support
part of the account, but it also introduced a contradiction. That contradiction prevented the
event from being dismissed as fatigue, coincidence or a simple technical fault.

By then, the pattern was deliberate. She asks, 'Am I beautiful?' Paul replies politely, but the
street repeats and returns him to the same vending machine. The protagonist tried a reasonable
safety measure rather than moving deeper into danger. The measure failed in a specific way,
showing that the threat could anticipate normal human responses.

The central rule was broken shortly afterward. She removes the mask, revealing a mouth cut
toward both ears, and asks again. From this point onward, each decision carried a visible
consequence. The recurring sound or object returned whenever the protagonist moved closer to
understanding the rule.

The discovery changed the meaning of everything that came before. Remembering the legend, Paul
gives an ambiguous answer and throws wrapped sweets into the road as a distraction. The
discovery connected the present incident to earlier victims or earlier versions of the same
legend. It also revealed that the familiar warning had been misunderstood.

There was no perfect solution, only a narrow opportunity. The woman follows without walking,
appearing in reflective surfaces ahead of him. The escape plan relied on observation rather
than physical strength. One small inconsistency offered the only way to tell the genuine path
from the trap.

The threat recognised the plan almost immediately. Paul enters a police box, but the officer
inside has the same hidden smile. The threat reacted by using a trusted voice, memory or
familiar place. That made the final choice emotional as well as practical.

For several minutes, escape appeared possible. He realises the question is not about
appearance; it forces the victim to agree to a version of reality she controls. The immediate
danger ended without a complete explanation. Authorities, relatives or witnesses could verify
only fragments of what had happened.

The official account ended there. Paul answers, 'You are not asking me,' breaking eye contact
and addressing the empty street. The last piece of evidence changed the meaning of the apparent
escape. It suggested that the event had continued after the protagonist believed it was over.

One final detail was discovered later. The woman vanishes, but every photograph of Paul taken
afterward shows a masked figure waiting behind him. No reliable account explains what happened
next. The surviving evidence supports more than one interpretation, and none of them is
comforting.

The account is still repeated because it leaves one question unresolved. Did Paul Reeves defeat
the thing at the centre of the story, or did the encounter simply teach it how to remain
hidden? The final warning is therefore simple: when the same impossible detail appears twice,
do not wait for a third occurrence to prove that it is real.
