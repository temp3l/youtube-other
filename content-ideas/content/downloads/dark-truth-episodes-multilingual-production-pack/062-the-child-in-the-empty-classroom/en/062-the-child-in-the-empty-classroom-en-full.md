# Narration Script


Clara Evans encountered a child writing tomorrow's date on a dusty blackboard. What seemed like an isolated event became a pattern that knew exactly how to follow.

Clara Evans was a school caretaker when the story began in an old primary school. The first impossible detail was a child writing tomorrow's date on a dusty blackboard. It seemed harmless enough to dismiss, which gave the danger time to grow.
The first event seemed explainable. Clara sees a child through the window of a classroom locked for years. The exact time and physical evidence were recorded, making the account difficult to dismiss.
The next incident made coincidence less convincing. The room is empty when she enters, but fresh chalk covers the board. A second witness or recording supported part of the story while creating a contradiction.
By then, the pattern was deliberate. The same child appears on old class photographs from different decades. The recurring motif returned closer than before and reacted to the protagonist's decisions.
The danger became personal shortly afterward. Each night, a new name is added beneath tomorrow's date. From that moment, the threat stopped hiding and began changing the environment.
The discovery changed the meaning of what came before. One listed teacher disappears before morning. Earlier events now looked like preparation rather than isolated disturbances.
There was no safe solution, only a narrow opportunity. Clara learns the classroom was sealed after a fire that never appeared in school records. The plan depended on recognising one stable rule instead of overpowering the threat.
The threat reacted immediately. She finds a register containing names of children officially never enrolled. A trusted voice, memory or place was used to make the wrong choice feel safe.
For a moment, escape appeared possible. The child asks Clara to finish taking attendance. Survival left evidence that could be verified only in fragments.
The immediate danger seemed to end there. Clara reads every forgotten name aloud, and the classroom door finally opens. The official explanation covered practical facts but not the impossible detail.
One final detail was discovered later. The next day, one empty desk appears in Clara's office with her name on it. That final piece of evidence changed the meaning of the apparent escape.
That is why the story is still repeated. The disturbing question is not only what happened to Clara Evans. It is whether the final warning describes a danger that ended—or a method the danger now uses to find the next person.
