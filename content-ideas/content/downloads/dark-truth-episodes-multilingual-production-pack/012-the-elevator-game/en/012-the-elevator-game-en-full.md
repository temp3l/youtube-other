# Narration Script

Emma completed the button sequence exactly. On the tenth floor, the doors opened onto a city
with no lights and a red sky.

Emma Shaw was a graduate student testing an urban legend for a podcast when the story began in
a nearly empty twenty-storey apartment tower. The first reports did not describe an obvious
monster or violent attack. They described an elevator chime sounding from floors that do not
exist. That detail mattered because it returned at moments when no ordinary source could be
found.

Most frightening stories become exaggerated after they are repeated. This one became more
precise. Dates, rooms, recordings and witness statements differed in minor ways, but the
central sequence remained the same. A warning was ignored, an impossible pattern appeared, and
the person at the centre of it survived with evidence that made survival difficult to
celebrate.

The first event seemed harmless enough to rationalise. Emma records herself following the
ritual alone after midnight. The setting was described in enough detail to make the account
testable: the time, weather and nearby objects were all recorded. Later, those ordinary details
became important because several of them changed without explanation.

The next incident removed some of that comfort. At the fifth floor, a woman enters but never
presses a button. A second witness or recording appeared to support part of the account, but it
also introduced a contradiction. That contradiction prevented the event from being dismissed as
fatigue, coincidence or a simple technical fault.

By then, the pattern was deliberate. The rules say Emma must not speak to or look at the woman.
The protagonist tried a reasonable safety measure rather than moving deeper into danger. The
measure failed in a specific way, showing that the threat could anticipate normal human
responses.

The central rule was broken shortly afterward. The elevator travels upward although the display
counts backward. From this point onward, each decision carried a visible consequence. The
recurring sound or object returned whenever the protagonist moved closer to understanding the
rule.

The discovery changed the meaning of everything that came before. On the tenth floor, Emma
enters a silent version of her city where every window is dark. The discovery connected the
present incident to earlier victims or earlier versions of the same legend. It also revealed
that the familiar warning had been misunderstood.

There was no perfect solution, only a narrow opportunity. Her phone shows messages she will
send several hours in the future warning herself not to return. The escape plan relied on
observation rather than physical strength. One small inconsistency offered the only way to tell
the genuine path from the trap.

The threat recognised the plan almost immediately. The silent woman follows and reveals that
she is a previous version of Emma who failed the return sequence. The threat reacted by using a
trusted voice, memory or familiar place. That made the final choice emotional as well as
practical.

For several minutes, escape appeared possible. Only one passenger can use the elevator back.
The immediate danger ended without a complete explanation. Authorities, relatives or witnesses
could verify only fragments of what had happened.

The official account ended there. Emma tricks the system by stopping between floors and opening
the emergency hatch. The last piece of evidence changed the meaning of the apparent escape. It
suggested that the event had continued after the protagonist believed it was over.

One final detail was discovered later. She returns home, but every elevator now opens for her
before she presses the call button. No reliable account explains what happened next. The
surviving evidence supports more than one interpretation, and none of them is comforting.

The account is still repeated because it leaves one question unresolved. Did Emma Shaw defeat
the thing at the centre of the story, or did the encounter simply teach it how to remain
hidden? The final warning is therefore simple: when the same impossible detail appears twice,
do not wait for a third occurrence to prove that it is real.
