# Narration Script

The old woman gave Nina one instruction: keep the candle burning until sunrise. When the flame
died, something outside stood on two legs.

Nina Falk was a wildlife photographer sheltering from a blizzard when the story began in an
isolated mountain cottage in the Carpathians. The first reports did not describe an obvious
monster or violent attack. They described a black candle whose flame bends toward the forest.
That detail mattered because it returned at moments when no ordinary source could be found.

Most frightening stories become exaggerated after they are repeated. This one became more
precise. Dates, rooms, recordings and witness statements differed in minor ways, but the
central sequence remained the same. A warning was ignored, an impossible pattern appeared, and
the person at the centre of it survived with evidence that made survival difficult to
celebrate.

The first event seemed harmless enough to rationalise. Nina becomes stranded near a remote
village after photographing unusually large wolf tracks. The setting was described in enough
detail to make the account testable: the time, weather and nearby objects were all recorded.
Later, those ordinary details became important because several of them changed without
explanation.

The next incident removed some of that comfort. An elderly widow offers shelter and lights a
black beeswax candle marked with names. A second witness or recording appeared to support part
of the account, but it also introduced a contradiction. That contradiction prevented the event
from being dismissed as fatigue, coincidence or a simple technical fault.

By then, the pattern was deliberate. The widow explains that the flame does not repel the
creature; it reminds it which body belongs to it. The protagonist tried a reasonable safety
measure rather than moving deeper into danger. The measure failed in a specific way, showing
that the threat could anticipate normal human responses.

The central rule was broken shortly afterward. Scratching circles the cottage while a human
voice begs to be let inside. From this point onward, each decision carried a visible
consequence. The recurring sound or object returned whenever the protagonist moved closer to
understanding the rule.

The discovery changed the meaning of everything that came before. Nina sees a man in the snow
wearing the missing ranger's coat, but his knees bend the wrong way. The discovery connected
the present incident to earlier victims or earlier versions of the same legend. It also
revealed that the familiar warning had been misunderstood.

There was no perfect solution, only a narrow opportunity. The candle gutters whenever Nina
speaks the ranger's name. The escape plan relied on observation rather than physical strength.
One small inconsistency offered the only way to tell the genuine path from the trap.

The threat recognised the plan almost immediately. A draft extinguishes it, and the widow
begins transforming—not into a wolf, but back into a terrified human. The threat reacted by
using a trusted voice, memory or familiar place. That made the final choice emotional as well
as practical.

For several minutes, escape appeared possible. The creature outside was the widow's stolen
animal shape, which has hunted independently for decades. The immediate danger ended without a
complete explanation. Authorities, relatives or witnesses could verify only fragments of what
had happened.

The official account ended there. Nina relights the candle using the fireplace and forces the
two halves to merge before dawn. The last piece of evidence changed the meaning of the apparent
escape. It suggested that the event had continued after the protagonist believed it was over.

One final detail was discovered later. Months later, every photograph from that night shows
Nina casting a wolf-shaped shadow. No reliable account explains what happened next. The
surviving evidence supports more than one interpretation, and none of them is comforting.

The account is still repeated because it leaves one question unresolved. Did Nina Falk defeat
the thing at the centre of the story, or did the encounter simply teach it how to remain
hidden? The final warning is therefore simple: when the same impossible detail appears twice,
do not wait for a third occurrence to prove that it is real.
