# Narration Script

Sophie found a black rose on the counter each morning. Every person named on its tag died
before the flower opened.

Sophie Marek was a florist returning home after her grandmother's death when the story began in
a family-owned flower shop in Prague. The first reports did not describe an obvious monster or
violent attack. They described black petals appearing in locked rooms. That detail mattered
because it returned at moments when no ordinary source could be found.

Most frightening stories become exaggerated after they are repeated. This one became more
precise. Dates, rooms, recordings and witness statements differed in minor ways, but the
central sequence remained the same. A warning was ignored, an impossible pattern appeared, and
the person at the centre of it survived with evidence that made survival difficult to
celebrate.

The first event seemed harmless enough to rationalise. Sophie inherits a flower shop and
discovers a sealed basement greenhouse. The setting was described in enough detail to make the
account testable: the time, weather and nearby objects were all recorded. Later, those ordinary
details became important because several of them changed without explanation.

The next incident removed some of that comfort. A black rose appears with a handwritten name
tied to its stem. A second witness or recording appeared to support part of the account, but it
also introduced a contradiction. That contradiction prevented the event from being dismissed as
fatigue, coincidence or a simple technical fault.

By then, the pattern was deliberate. The named customer dies in an accident before sunset, and
the rose opens at the same moment. The protagonist tried a reasonable safety measure rather
than moving deeper into danger. The measure failed in a specific way, showing that the threat
could anticipate normal human responses.

The central rule was broken shortly afterward. More roses arrive, each predicting a death
connected to the shop. From this point onward, each decision carried a visible consequence. The
recurring sound or object returned whenever the protagonist moved closer to understanding the
rule.

The discovery changed the meaning of everything that came before. Sophie tries to destroy them,
but burned petals reappear inside her mouth while she sleeps. The discovery connected the
present incident to earlier victims or earlier versions of the same legend. It also revealed
that the familiar warning had been misunderstood.

There was no perfect solution, only a narrow opportunity. Her grandmother's ledger reveals that
the roses do not predict death; they choose who the shop will exchange for another life. The
escape plan relied on observation rather than physical strength. One small inconsistency
offered the only way to tell the genuine path from the trap.

The threat recognised the plan almost immediately. Sophie finds her own name on a flower that
has not yet bloomed. The threat reacted by using a trusted voice, memory or familiar place.
That made the final choice emotional as well as practical.

For several minutes, escape appeared possible. She learns the last exchange saved her as a
child by taking her father's life. The immediate danger ended without a complete explanation.
Authorities, relatives or witnesses could verify only fragments of what had happened.

The official account ended there. Rather than choose another victim, Sophie locks herself in
the greenhouse and cuts every root. The last piece of evidence changed the meaning of the
apparent escape. It suggested that the event had continued after the protagonist believed it
was over.

One final detail was discovered later. The roses die, but one black bud later appears in a
bouquet delivered to a newborn child. No reliable account explains what happened next. The
surviving evidence supports more than one interpretation, and none of them is comforting.

The account is still repeated because it leaves one question unresolved. Did Sophie Marek
defeat the thing at the centre of the story, or did the encounter simply teach it how to remain
hidden? The final warning is therefore simple: when the same impossible detail appears twice,
do not wait for a third occurrence to prove that it is real.
