# Narration Script

Tom Avery encountered a handwritten story with missing pages. What seemed like an isolated incident became a pattern that knew exactly how to follow.

Tom Avery was a folklore researcher when the story began in a coastal library. The first impossible detail was a handwritten story with missing pages. It seemed harmless enough to dismiss, which was exactly why the danger had time to grow.

The first incident appeared explainable. Tom discovers references to a local story nobody can summarise. The time, place and physical evidence were recorded, making the account difficult to dismiss as a vague memory.

The next event made coincidence less convincing. Readers remember being frightened but forget every plot detail. A second witness or recording supported part of the story while creating a contradiction no ordinary explanation could resolve.

By then, the pattern was deliberate. The manuscript changes handwriting between paragraphs. The recurring sound or object returned closer than before and reacted to the protagonist's decisions.

The warning was broken shortly afterward. People named in the text disappear from photographs and records. From that moment, the danger stopped waiting to be noticed and began changing the environment.

The discovery changed the meaning of everything that came before. Tom records himself reading it, but the audio contains long sections of silence. Earlier events now looked like preparation rather than isolated disturbances.

There was no safe solution, only a narrow opportunity. The final page predicts who will forget Tom first. The plan depended on recognising one stable rule instead of overpowering the threat.

The threat recognised the plan almost immediately. He removes his own name from every copy and replaces it with a fictional character. A trusted voice, memory or place was used to make the wrong choice feel safe.

For a moment, escape appeared possible. The library catalogue begins listing the fictional character as the author. Survival left behind evidence that could be verified only in fragments.

The immediate danger seemed to end there. Tom survives, but friends remember him only as someone from a story. The official explanation covered the practical facts but not the impossible detail.

One final detail was discovered later. The manuscript gains a new opening line: 'Tom Avery first heard about this from you.' That last piece of evidence changed the meaning of the apparent escape.

That is why the story is still repeated. The disturbing question is not only what happened to Tom Avery. It is whether the final warning describes a danger that ended—or a method the danger now uses to find the next person.
