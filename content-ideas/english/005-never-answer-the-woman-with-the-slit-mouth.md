# Never Answer the Woman With the Slit Mouth

During the spring of 1979, the school journey changed for children across parts of Japan.

They were told not to walk home alone.

Teachers escorted groups through the streets. Parents waited outside school gates. Some children carried whistles. Others hid candy in their pockets.

The reason was not an official police warning.

It was a rumor.

A woman with long hair and a surgical mask had supposedly begun approaching children on quiet streets.

She asked only one question:

**“Am I beautiful?”**

Anyone who answered incorrectly would never make it home.

But even the children who gave the correct answer were not safe.

Because beneath the mask, the woman's mouth had been cut from ear to ear.

Then she asked again:

**“What about now?”**

Her name was Kuchisake-Onna.

The Slit-Mouthed Woman.

## The First Encounter

The story is often told through the experience of a twelve-year-old boy named Hiroshi.

One misty evening, he remained at school later than usual.

By the time he left, the streets were nearly empty.

Only the hum of streetlights and distant traffic accompanied him.

Hiroshi knew the rumors.

One child claimed to have seen the woman near a park.

Another said his sister had been followed.

Adults dismissed the stories.

Still, Hiroshi walked faster.

Near a narrow side street, he heard footsteps behind him.

Slow.

Even.

Not fast enough to appear threatening.

Not slow enough to fall behind.

He looked over his shoulder.

A woman followed him.

She wore a long red coat. Black hair fell over her shoulders, and a white mask covered her face.

In Japan, masks were not unusual.

People wore them when ill or to avoid infection.

But something felt wrong.

The woman did not walk along one side of the pavement.

She followed Hiroshi's exact path.

When he moved left, she moved left.

When he accelerated, her footsteps accelerated.

Hiroshi turned into another street.

The footsteps followed.

Then came her voice.

“Excuse me.”

He stopped.

The woman stood only a few meters away.

Her head tilted.

“Am I beautiful?”

## The Samurai's Wife

One of the oldest versions of the legend reaches back to Japan's Heian period.

A powerful samurai was married to an extraordinarily beautiful woman.

She knew she was beautiful.

Some stories describe her as vain. Others portray her only as independent.

The samurai was jealous.

He believed she had betrayed him.

Whether he had any evidence does not matter to the legend.

One night, he lost control.

He drew his sword.

Then he cut her mouth from one ear to the other.

As she lay bleeding, he asked:

“Who will think you are beautiful now?”

The woman died.

Her anger remained.

She became a yōkai, a supernatural being neither entirely ghost nor demon.

Since then, she has wandered the streets, forcing strangers to answer the same question her husband answered with a blade.

Am I beautiful?

A simple no does not help.

Anyone who insults her is attacked.

But yes is not safe either.

Kuchisake-Onna does not want an honest answer.

She wants others to carry the same wound.

## The First Question

Hiroshi knew the rule.

To the first question, say yes.

At least that was what his classmates had told him.

“Yes,” he whispered. “You are beautiful.”

The woman did not move.

Then both hands rose to her face.

Her fingers touched the mask.

“And now?”

She pulled it down.

At first, Hiroshi could not understand what he saw.

The mouth did not end at the cheeks.

Dark scars extended toward both ears. The skin appeared pulled apart, turning the smile into a wound.

Too many teeth showed inside.

The woman smiled wider.

“Am I still beautiful?”

Hiroshi stepped back.

He had prepared for this question.

The correct answer was supposedly “normal.”

Not beautiful.

Not ugly.

Normal.

That answer would confuse her long enough to escape.

But now, standing before her, he could not speak.

The woman reached inside her coat.

She withdrew an enormous pair of scissors.

The blades were almost as long as his forearm.

“You do not need to be afraid,” she said.

“I can make you beautiful too.”

## A Legend That Changes

There are many versions of Kuchisake-Onna's origin.

In some, she is the samurai's wife.

In others, she is a modern woman disfigured during surgery.

One story describes a doctor whose hands shook while operating. His hair pomade had a powerful smell, and it was the last thing the woman remembered before losing her face.

That is why some children say shouting “pomade” repeatedly will drive her away.

Other stories describe a woman who escaped from an institution.

Or the victim of a violent gang.

In newer versions, radiation gave her additional toes and the ability to run faster than a normal human.

The contradictions do not weaken the legend.

They strengthen it.

Every generation creates its own Kuchisake-Onna.

She no longer hides behind a silk cloth.

She wears a modern mask.

She no longer waits only on forest paths.

She appears near schools, stations, parks, and apartment buildings.

She looks like somebody you might pass every day.

## The Escape Rules

Hiroshi remembered all the rules.

Throw candy.

Ask whether she thinks you are beautiful.

Say you have an urgent appointment.

Shout “pomade” six times.

The problem was that nobody knew which rule belonged to which version.

Perhaps “normal” was correct.

Perhaps it made her angry.

Perhaps candy distracted her.

Perhaps pomade reminded her of the doctor.

Or perhaps the word attracted her.

The woman raised the scissors.

Hiroshi reached into his schoolbag.

His fingers found a packet of sweets.

He threw it at her feet.

She looked down.

For one second, she seemed distracted.

Hiroshi ran.

Behind him, the scissors snapped shut.

A hard metallic sound.

Then the footsteps began.

She did not run like a normal person.

Between two steps, she seemed to cross several meters.

Hiroshi turned a corner.

When he looked back, the road was empty.

He kept running until he reached a small shop.

The owner let the terrified boy hide behind the counter.

No woman was found outside.

Only later did Hiroshi's mother discover a long cut across the back of his jacket.

It ran from one shoulder to the other.

## The Panic of 1979

During the spring and summer of 1979, rumors about Kuchisake-Onna spread through Japan.

Children told one another where she had last been seen.

Near a school.

In a park.

At a station.

Between two houses.

The descriptions were similar.

A woman.

Long dark hair.

A mask.

Sometimes a red coat.

And scissors, a knife, or a sickle.

The story spread without social media.

Children repeated it in schoolyards. Local media amplified it. Every retelling added new details.

At some schools, children were sent home only in groups.

Teachers and parents escorted them.

In certain areas, police reportedly increased patrols.

Perhaps no real woman existed.

Perhaps fear transformed every masked stranger into a monster.

But children began reporting details they should not have shared.

The same high voice.

The same rusted scissors.

A woman who did not appear in shop-window reflections.

A figure that vanished when confronted by stairs.

That produced another rule:

Kuchisake-Onna cannot use stairs.

Nobody knew why.

## The Girl at the Crossing

During the panic, a ten-year-old girl allegedly encountered a masked woman near a railway crossing.

The woman stood beside the closed barrier.

Although it was raining, she carried no umbrella.

Her clothes were completely dry.

The girl tried to pass.

“Am I beautiful?” the woman asked.

The child had heard the stories.

“You look normal,” she answered.

The woman became still.

For several seconds, the answer seemed to work.

Then she leaned closer.

“Normal?”

Something moved beneath the mask.

“What is normal about me?”

The barrier opened.

The girl ran across the tracks.

The woman did not follow.

Relieved, the child looked back.

Kuchisake-Onna remained on the other side.

Then a train passed between them.

When the final carriage cleared, the woman stood directly in front of the girl.

She had not crossed the tracks.

She had simply appeared.

The child remembered another rule.

“I have an important appointment,” she said.

Kuchisake-Onna paused.

Then she bowed politely.

“Forgive me.”

The girl ran home.

That night, she woke to tapping against her bedroom window.

The apartment was on the third floor.

Outside the glass floated a woman in a red coat.

She was no longer wearing a mask.

## The Second Answer

The consequences of answering incorrectly differ from story to story.

Anyone who says no is killed.

Anyone who says yes after the mask is removed receives the same mouth.

Some versions say men are beheaded.

Women become new Kuchisake-Onna.

If that version is true, there is not one monster.

There is a spreading curse.

Every new victim wanders the streets and asks the same questions.

That may explain why the woman was reportedly seen in several cities at once.

There was never only one.

The most disturbing stories concern those who escaped.

Some later developed itching at the corners of their mouths.

Fine red lines appeared on their cheeks.

Each night, the lines extended closer to the ears.

Until one morning, they woke with a smile they had never made.

## The Records

In 2007, another story spread.

A man allegedly found old records concerning a mentally ill woman who chased children during the 1970s.

According to the documents, she died after being struck by a car.

The crash disfigured her face in a way resembling Kuchisake-Onna.

Some people treated this as the explanation for the panic.

A real woman had frightened children.

After her death, she became a ghost story.

The records were never reliably verified.

And even if they existed, there was a problem.

Stories of the slit-mouthed woman were older.

Perhaps the accident was later added to the legend.

Perhaps the woman imitated the myth.

Or perhaps she had survived an encounter with it.

## The Red Mask

In 2004, a similar panic reportedly appeared in South Korea.

There, people spoke of a woman with a red mask.

Again, children warned one another.

Again, an injured woman supposedly approached people in the street.

The legend had left Japan.

It adapted.

That is what makes Kuchisake-Onna unusual.

Many ghosts are tied to one house, forest, or death site.

She waits anywhere a person is alone.

An empty road is enough.

A mask is enough.

A question is enough.

And the more often people repeat her story, the easier she becomes to recognize.

Or the easier it becomes for something else to imitate her.

## The Third Answer

Hiroshi did not speak about his encounter for many years.

He grew up, moved away, and convinced himself that he had met an ordinary woman.

Perhaps childhood fear had transformed the mask into a wound.

Perhaps the cut on his jacket came from a fence.

Nearly thirty years later, Hiroshi had a daughter of his own.

One evening, she came home late from school.

She was pale and said nothing during dinner.

Hiroshi asked what happened.

The girl placed a packet of candy on the table.

“A woman gave me these.”

“What woman?”

“She wore a white mask.”

His body went cold.

The daughter explained that the woman asked whether she was beautiful.

The girl said yes.

Then the woman removed the mask.

“What did you say next?”

“I told her she looked like the woman you ran away from when you were a child.”

Hiroshi had never told his daughter that story.

“And then?”

“She laughed.”

The girl pushed the candy toward him.

A message had been written on the back:

**“This time, I will not ask you in the street.”**

That night, Hiroshi checked every door and window.

Shortly after midnight, he heard movement in the bathroom.

The soft rustle of a mask.

The room was empty.

Only the mirror was fogged.

A question had been written across it:

**“Am I beautiful?”**

Hiroshi wiped the words away.

Behind him, his daughter's voice asked:

“And now?”

He turned.

The girl stood in the doorway wearing a white mask.

Slowly, she lifted her hands toward the straps.

Hiroshi did not know what answer would save him.

Yes.

No.

Normal.

Pomade.

A question.

An appointment.

Perhaps none of the rules had ever worked.

Perhaps Kuchisake-Onna deliberately allowed some people to escape so they would spread her story.

So every child would know the questions.

So fear of her would never die.

His daughter removed the mask.

Her face was unharmed.

Hiroshi exhaled.

Then, inside the mirror behind her, a second mouth opened.

A woman stood in the reflection.

Directly behind him.

Her smile reached both ears.

She leaned close and whispered:

“You never answered me.”

The apartment was empty the next morning.

Hiroshi and his daughter had vanished.

Police found only a white mask and a large pair of scissors.

Three sentences were written on the mirror:

**“Yes is wrong.”**

**“No is wrong.”**

**“Silence is also an answer.”**

Children now tell one more rule.

If Kuchisake-Onna asks whether she is beautiful, do not answer and do not run.

Pretend you did not hear her.

Nobody knows whether it works.

Because sometimes she does not stand in front of you.

Sometimes you see her only in a dark shop window.

Or in the black screen of your phone.

She stands in the reflection behind you.

First, she wears the mask.

Then she removes it.

And even if you do not turn around, you hear her second question directly beside your ear:

**“What about now?”**
